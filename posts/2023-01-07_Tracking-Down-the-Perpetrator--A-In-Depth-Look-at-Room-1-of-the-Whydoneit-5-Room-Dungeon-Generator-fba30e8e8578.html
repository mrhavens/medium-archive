Tracking Down the Perpetrator: A In-Depth Look at Room 1 of the Whydoneit 5-Room Dungeon Generator

Examining the crime scene and following the clues

This generator is designed to help you create your own crime-solving adventure, where the characters must investigate a series of interconnected rooms and gather clues to solve a mystery.

Room 1 is the starting point of the adventure, where the characters may encounter the victim of the crime and begin to uncover the first clues.

To use the generator, simply roll the appropriate dice for each element of the room, and use the resulting numbers to determine the details of the room. For example, if you roll a 4 on the D4 for the type of crime that was committed, the crime was vandalism. If you roll a 6 on the D6 for the location of the crime scene, the crime scene is in a basement.

Using the generator, you can create a unique and exciting crime-solving adventure every time, with endless possibilities for clues, suspects, and twists. So grab your dice and get ready to unravel the mystery in Room 1: The crime scene!

Room 1: The crime scene

This is the crime scene and the starting point of your crime-solving adventure! In this room, the characters will encounter the victim of the crime and begin to uncover the first clues that will help them solve the mystery. Using the generator, you can determine the details of the crime scene, including the type of crime that was committed, the location of the crime scene, the number of clues and suspects, and the condition of the victim.

As the characters explore the crime scene and gather evidence, they will need to use their wits and skills to piece together the events leading up to the crime and identify the perpetrator. With each roll of the dice, new challenges and opportunities will arise, and the characters will have to adapt their strategy and follow the clues wherever they lead.

Are you ready to solve the mystery and bring the perpetrator to justice? Step into Room 1: The crime scene and see where the investigation takes you!

D4: The type of crime that was committed

The type of crime that was committed is a crucial element in any crime-solving adventure, as it determines the nature of the investigation and the potential motives and suspects. With the D4 roll for the type of crime that was committed, you can determine the focus of the characters’ investigation as they try to solve the mystery.

The possible results of the roll include murder, theft, assault, and vandalism. Each of these crimes has its own characteristics and consequences and can lead to different lines of investigation and suspect profiles. For example, if the crime was murder, the characters will need to identify the victim and any potential witnesses, while if the crime was theft, they will need to determine the value and nature of the stolen property.

Using the D4 roll for the type of crime that was committed, you can add depth and realism to your crime-solving adventure and keep your players guessing about the next twist in the story.

1) Murder — the intentional and unlawful taking of another person’s life.
2) Theft — the act of unlawfully taking personal property without using threats or violence.
3) Assault — the physical attack or threat of physical harm against a person or persons.
4) Vandalism — the malicious or intentional destruction of property that does not belong to them.

D6: Murder

1) Murder by poisoning — the act of intentionally giving a person poison or other toxic substance that causes their death.
2) Strangulation — the act of using hands, arms, or a ligature to compress someone’s throat, cutting off their air supply and causing them to suffocate.
3) Ranged Attack — an act of violence where an assailant uses weapons such as guns and bows and arrows to kill their victim from a distance.
4) Stabbing — the act of using a sharp instrument to pierce the skin or other body part of another in order to cause injury or death.
5) Bludgeoning — using blunt objects like bats, clubs, hammers or fists with intent of inflicting physical harm on another person resulting in their death.
6) Suffocation — the inability to breath resulting from obstruction of air passages leading to impairment or cessation of respiration caused by another person resulting in death.

D6: Theft

1) Stealing precious gems from a guarded vault — the act of taking gems or other valuables from a defended location, usually involving breaking into the vault or overcoming security measures.
2) Rescuing a cursed object from a hidden location — a daring act of stealing an item that has been cursed and requires special skills to access its secret hiding spot.
3) Swiping food, tools, or weapons from unsuspecting victims — taking advantage of people’s unawareness by slyly stealing items without them noticing.
4) Joyriding in a magical conveyance — riding uninvited in some form of supernatural vehicle such as a flying carpet, enchanted carriage or anything else that is spellbound.
5) Pilfering an enchanted scroll from an eldritch library — stealthily taking a magical scroll that contains secrets that are otherwise guarded by ancient knowledge found in strange and mysterious libraries.
6) Plundering a sacred temple — the act of robbery and desecration against sites of religious importance resulting in loss and destruction of property.

D6: Assault

1) Assaulting a city guard — the act of physically or verbally attacking an officer of the law resulting in injury or fear.
2) Attacking a nobleman — the act of engaging in physical combat with someone of high social status caused by hatred or envy.
3) Battery of a helpless citizen — causing personal harm to a person who is unable to defend themselves, usually resulting from malice or cruelty.
4) Reckless endangerment of another person — putting someone’s safety in danger through risky behavior or negligence without regard for the possible consequences.
5) Unlawful detention of another person — restraining someone against their will, violating their rights, and depriving them of freedom without having legal authority to do so.
6) Intimidation or coercion of a witness — applying pressure on people to make them testify under false pretenses, change testimony, withhold evidence or give false information during legal proceedings against their will.

D6: Vandalism

1) Creating magical graffiti that defiles or blasphemes sacred sites and symbols.
2) Destroying magical runes or symbols of power, such as those found on ancient artifacts or structures.
3) Sacrilege against religious icons or relics by desecrating and mutilating them with dark enchantments.
4) Damaging public property with magic in order to cause physical harm or spread chaos and fear.
5) Defacing works of art with curses, hexes, psychic attacks and other malicious magical effects.
6) Utilizing powerful spells and rituals to summon fireballs, tornadoes, storms, plagues and other forms of destruction.

D6: The location of the crime scene

The location of the crime scene is an important factor in any crime-solving adventure, as it can provide clues about the nature of the crime, the motive of the perpetrator, and the potential witnesses or suspects. With the D6 roll for the location of the crime scene, you can determine the type of space where the characters first encounter the victim and begin their investigation.

The possible results of the roll include a bedroom, kitchen, living room, office, workshop, or basement. Each of these locations presents different challenges and opportunities for the characters as they try to solve the mystery. For example, a bedroom might contain personal belongings and clues that reveal the victim’s relationships and habits, while a workshop might contain tools or materials that are relevant to the crime.

Using the D6 roll for the location of the crime scene, you can add depth and realism to your crime-solving adventure and keep your players guessing about the next twist in the story.

1) Bedroom: A private room typically used for sleeping.
2) Kitchen: A room used for cooking and food preparation.
3) Living room: A room in a dwelling used for relaxation and entertaining guests.
4) Office: An area designated for carrying out administrative or professional duties.
5) Workshop: A place equipped with tools and materials for practical work, such as carpentry or mechanics.
6) Basement: The lowest story of a building, often partially or entirely below ground level.

With the D8 roll for the number of suspects, you can determine the scope of the investigation and the complexity of the case.

D10: The number of clues found at the crime scene

Clues are pieces of evidence or information that can help the characters solve a crime or mystery. They can be physical items, such as a weapon with the victim’s blood on it or a witness who saw the perpetrator at the scene, or they can be more abstract, such as a motive for the crime or a connection between the victim and the perpetrator. In a role-playing game, clues can be used to help the players understand what happened and who is responsible and can provide them with a sense of progress as they work to solve the case.

4–6) several clues
7–8) many clues
9–10) an overwhelming amount of clues

D12: The state of the crime scene

The state of the crime scene can be an indicator of what happened and help investigators piece together a more complete picture. Depending on the severity, it can range from organized to chaotic with evidence strewn about, signs of forced entry, premeditated or spontaneous actions taken at the scene, or even evidence removed in an attempt to thwart the investigation. Sometimes crime is interrupted before it is completed and no criminal activity is spotted during the initial inspection.

1) Organized — well-organized, neat, and in order
2) Chaotic — evidence disturbed, signs of a struggle
3) Prepared — premeditated actions were taken at the scene
4) Reactive — spontaneous actions are taken in response to provocation or threats
5) Undetermined — no clear indication of what happened at the scene
6) Messy — debris scattered around the area, along with signs of forced entry
7) Concealed — evidence left to avoid detection by law enforcement
8) Evidence removed — evidence removed in an attempt to thwart the investigation
9) Interrupted — crime was stopped before it reached completion
10) No activity — no criminal activity spotted during the initial inspection
