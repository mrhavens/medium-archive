Narcissism and Narrative Therapy: A Path to Healing

For men who have survived narcissistic abuse — understanding the role of self-story and how narrative therapy can help narcissists heal

Introduction

As a survivor of narcissistic abuse, you know firsthand the destructive power of a grandiose self-story.

For example, my narcissistic ex once had very strong opinions about her spiritual abilities, claiming she regularly received prophetic words from God and that certain church denominations like mine, were for beginners.

These prophetic words most often involved schemes to make money.

Paradoixidely, she also told me that God told her that she would never have to work.

I told her that while I may not hear from God as she did, it was also a pretty good sign that God wanted us to be poor.

I was just two years into a Ph.D. fellowship at the time. Money wasn’t exactly growing on trees.

Although I humored her notions about her prophetic gifts, I knew she was using her self-narrative as a prophet of God as a means to manipulate me into quitting my Ph.D. program so that I would have the time to work a much more lucrative job.

As any Ph.D. will tell you, you don’t spend five to seven years getting a Ph.D. and being subjected to the abusive whims of a pathologically narcissistic advisor, just so that you can make more money.
Nope. Earning a Ph.D. is more often an altruistic pursuit that helps you exchange wealth for… well… a five to seven year gap in your resume that proves you’re willing to work for less money than your competition.
In our modern world, it’s an obsolete credential.
Any Ph.D. that says differently is probably a narcissist with tenure.

I told her as much, but I wanted one anyway. I didn’t care about money. It was just a realization of a life-long dream.

I simply loved learning, and I wanted the proof in a secure, inaccessible fireproof box that sat under a mountain of crap in my closet, alongside the rest of my degrees.

Is that too much to ask?

Ever since I explained this dream to her, she dedicated the remainder of our relationship gently pressuring me to abandon it, without ever saying so for fear of having an unsupportive soundbite that could be used against her.

She liked to project these fears onto me, you see.

And eventually, abandon it I did, because she wanted a divorce that I couldn’t afford on a defunded Ph.D. student’s salary.

Keep in mind, we got married after one year after my being awarded a fully funded Ph.D. fellowship.

Anyway…

I guess I’ll try for another Ph.D. later.

In the meantime, I’ve decided to write for a living.

Makes sense, given that writing is all I’ve been doing since 2016 anyway.

And now, I’m even more broke than I was as a Ph.D. student.

But, I digress…

Narcissistic individuals may have an exaggerated sense of their own importance and a lack of empathy for others, leading to problems in relationships and achieving goals.

My problem has always been with codependency. At least, in the beginning.

I’ve enabled the narcissists in my life several times.

I’ve tried to fix them, love them, and support them…

I’ve become their source of narcissistic supply and even operated as a flying monkey.

I’ve been a party in undermining the lives of those that stood up to them.

That makes me codependent.

Once I realized that there was a problem and I begin to set boundaries, that’s when all hell breaks loose in the relationship.

But let’s keep one thing in mind.

Narcissists are individuals who are struggling and in need of healing, just like anyone else.

Seriously… I’m probably the first person you’ve read that has said that.

It’s true.

I’ll say it again.

Narcissists are individuals who are struggling and in need of healing, just like anyone else.

They just won’t ever get the healing they need because they’re too far gone.

Most of them, anyway.

For a defeated narcissist who feels compelled to seek help due to a total narcissistic collapse and terminal loss of a critical source of supply, there may be hope.

I just wouldn’t count on them ever seeking help… ever.

Ever… ever… ever.

Not without their life imploding, with everything they know taken away from them until they hit absolute rock bottom and have no choice but to seek help.

Often, a narcissist would rather face suicide than receive help from anyone they couldn’t manipulate.

But, on the off chance they hit rock bottom and choose meaningful, life-changing therapy, there are options for healing.

One potential path to healing is through narrative therapy.

The role of narrative in narcissistic personalities

The self-narrative is an important part of our identity, as it helps us to make sense of who we are, our purpose in life, and our aspirations.

If your self-narrative is well supported by facts and truth, then there’s no problem.

However, when the self-narrative breaks down and is based on delusions and false narratives, as it often does in narcissism, it can cause problems in an individual’s life.

In narcissism, the self-story is often grandiose and not based on reality, which can lead to difficulties in relationships and achieving goals.

This grandiose self-story can be destructive, not just for those around the narcissistic individual, but for the narcissist themselves.

But there is hope for healing through narrative therapy.

How narrative therapy can help narcissists

Narrative therapy is a type of psychotherapy that helps individuals to examine and change the stories they tell themselves about their lives.

It can be an effective treatment for individuals with narcissistic personalities, who may struggle with problems related to their self-narratives.

In narcissism, the self-story is often grandiose and not based on reality, which can lead to difficulties in relationships and achieving goals.

However, through narrative therapy, narcissists can learn to co-author a new narrative that is based on reality and improve their lives.

This process involves working with a therapist to assess their own history and skills in order to create a narrative that is helpful in some way.

The therapist listens and guides the patient as they work to create a new narrative, and encourages them to separate their problems from their identity.

By doing this, narcissists can gain greater control over their problems and exercise new skills to improve their lives.

Conclusion

Narrative therapy offers a promising approach for narcissists seeking to heal and grow.

By working with a therapist to co-author a new narrative based on reality, these individuals can gain a greater understanding of themselves and their problems, and develop more accurate and helpful self-narratives.

As a survivor of narcissistic abuse, understanding this process can help you to better comprehend and cope with the challenges of interacting with a narcissistic individual.

While healing from narcissistic abuse is a difficult and ongoing process, it is possible to reclaim your narrative and move forward in a healthier and more fulfilling way.
