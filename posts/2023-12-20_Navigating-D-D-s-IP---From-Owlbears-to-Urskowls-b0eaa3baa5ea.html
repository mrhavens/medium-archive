Navigating D&D’s IP — From Owlbears to Urskowls

Navigating the fine line between respecting trademarks and creating original D&D content as third party creators.

Dungeons & Dragons features a rich mythical bestiary, but legally referencing certain iconic creatures poses challenges. For home campaigns, DMs enjoy substantial creative freedom to borrow established content under personal use rights. However, public commercial works don’t enjoy the same permissions. As third-party publishers, we must tread carefully around trademarked product identities.

While the D&D SRD and OGL allow some open use of defined ruleset terms, aspects like monster names remain protected IP. For example, the infamous “owlbear”, while a D&D original, cannot be directly referenced without permission due to its long-secured trademark status. Even subtly alluding to owlbears risks creative works feeling more derivative than inventively transformative.

Introducing the Urskowl

So when conceptualizing dangerous owl/bear hybrids stalking primeval forests in my supplements, rather than leverage D&D’s owlbear fame, I crafted a wholly original creature — the urskowl.

Evoking mythic roots like griffins with lion/eagle fusion symbolism, the urskowl combines the nighttime hunting prowess of oversized owls with the fierce power of spirit bears. With ossified feathers forming mask-like facial plates and rear talons equally adept at perching, pouncing and disemboweling prey, the urskowl occupies an apex role in its isolated woodland domains unchanged since ancient eras.

While no less lethal than similar monstrosities like owlbears, tales of urskowls remain obscure due to their reclusive habitats and the few desperate folk who venture near their nests. Those oft-gory yet intriguing mysteries are precisely what we shall unravel in this beastiary — delving deep into the behaviors, abilities and deadly majesty of scourges most call out of pure legend or madness.

The mythos begins anew here. Just beware the silhouette circling silently above the shadowed trees at night’s fall!
