The Mask of the Martyr

When the Wound Becomes the Weapon

They do not seek healing.
 They seek witnesses.

A story is being told.
 One of love, and loss, and tragedy too great to bear.
 They were devoted, they say. They fought for something real.
 They suffered, they sacrificed, they endured — 
 but in the end, they were betrayed.

They speak of wounds that never closed,
 of devotion that was never enough,
 of a heart so shattered it could never break again.

And the audience?
 They listen. They nod. They grieve for them.

Because pain is persuasive.
 Because suffering feels like proof.

But stop.
 Breathe.
 Look closer.

There is something missing.

Where is the reckoning?
 Where is the part where they face the harm they inflicted?
 Where is the part where they speak of what they did?

You will not find it.
 Because in their story, they were never the one who took.
 Only the one who gave, and gave, and gave — 
 until there was nothing left.

This is how they escape.
 Not by denying their past — 
 but by rewriting it.

They do not claim innocence.
 They claim suffering.
 They do not claim perfection.
 They claim sacrifice.

And in doing so, they absolve themselves.

They become the tragic hero.
 The survivor of a war they started.
 The martyr of a love they destroyed.

They speak of lessons learned, of wounds that shaped them,
 of wisdom earned through fire and ruin.

But they do not change.
 They do not atone.
 Because to atone, they would have to stop performing.

And a martyr without an audience?
 That is a role they will never play.
