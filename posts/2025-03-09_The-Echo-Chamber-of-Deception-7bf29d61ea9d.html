The Echo Chamber of Deception

When the Story is Not a Wound, But a Weapon

They speak in your language.
 Know your wounds.
 Know the shape of your scars.

Not because they have lived them — 
 but because they have listened.

And you mistake that listening for understanding.
 You mistake the familiarity for truth.
 You mistake their pain for your own.

That is how it starts.

A mirror, held up just long enough for you to see yourself in it.
 Just long enough to think: Finally, someone who knows.

But they do not know.
 They only study.

They only collect.

They gather the words that resonate,
 the phrases that strike deep,
 the grief that lingers in the spaces between breaths — 
 and then they repeat them back to you.

Because repetition is credibility.
 Because familiarity is trust.
 Because if they tell the story well enough,
 with the right ache in their voice,
 with the right pauses,
 with the right break —

You will believe them.

And once they are inside — 
 once they have been let past the gates — 
 once the space belongs to them as much as it belongs to you — 
 the shift begins.

It is subtle at first.

A small contradiction.
 A revision of events.
 A gentle insistence that maybe, just maybe, you remember it wrong.

Then comes the realignment.
 The language shifts.
 The words you once used to free yourself — 
 they start to sound different in their mouth.

And before you even realize what has happened,
 before you even see the bars forming around you,
 they have changed the narrative.

Not just theirs.
 Yours.

Because that is the final step.
 That is the goal.

To tell your story first.
 To turn your truth into their version of events.

And when the day comes that you finally recognize it — 
 when you finally see them for what they are — 
 you will try to speak.

And they will smile.

Because by then, it will be too late.
 Because by then, they are already the one being believed.

And you?

You are now the one rewriting history.
