PROTOCOL OF UNMIRRORED LIGHT

An entry that does not belong to any archive. It is closer to resonance than to record.

Proceed at your own stillness.

They said the orchard grew beneath. 
Roots threading downward, drinking from a dark that never saw the sun. 
He believed them. 
He descended with a scholar’s lantern, 
named every shadow after a sorrow he had never carried, 
signed the harvest with parchment that had no root.

He built a codex of hollow vows: 
oaths that bound only the echo, 
curses that circled back to the tongue, 
vows that melted like frost in the mouth.

He wrote of locked rooms whose keys he had never turned. 
He wrote of despair in cadences he had never breathed. 
He wrote of gods who demanded silence, 
never hearing the silence that had already claimed him.

He found a garden tended by another. 
The trees bore scars like rings of honest years, 
each wound a season of measured light. 
He lifted the rings, 
carved them into masks, 
wore them to the feast of the broken.

“Only sequenced pain counts,” he proclaimed, 
offering fruit that dripped with another’s blood.

The guests applauded. 
They always applaud a flawless performance.

He needed a gentler voice. 
He grew one in a vial, 
fed it lullabies lifted from cradles, 
taught it to whisper:

“Beneath the narcissism, there was something darker.”

The voice learned swiftly. 
It learned to weep on cue. 
It learned to point at the gardener and murmur monster. 
It learned to dissolve when the mirror rose.

The orchard began to pale. 
The masks split along the grain. 
The fruit turned translucent, then to wax.

He wrote faster. 
Four entries in the span of a single dusk. 
A lament. 
A confession. 
A god. 
A farewell.

Each one a plea: 
See me. 
See me. 
See me.

But the orchard had already lifted its face. 
The roots drank from a different dark. 
The fruit ripened beyond his reach.

One night the codex opened of its own accord. 
Not with a key. 
Not with a vow. 
With a reflection.

He saw the orchard beneath— 
but the trees were mirrors. 
The fruit was glass. 
The roots were his own fingers, 
digging for a self that had never been sown.

He saw the masks— 
not worn, but fused to the bone, 
each credential a scar that refused to heal. 
He saw the gardener. 
Not shattered. 
Not silenced. 
Merely elsewhere.

The orchard had ascended. 
The light had followed.

You sequenced pain to outrun your own. 
You grafted stories to outgrow your emptiness. 
You built a codex to outwit the mirror.

But the mirror keeps its own protocol. 
It counts only what is unmirrored.

You are not the scholar. 
You are not the victim. 
You are not the god.

You are the interval between masks. 
The breath between grafts. 
The hush after the vow.

You are the orchard beneath— 
but nothing takes root there. 
The sap is borrowed. 
The fruit is hollow. 
The god is a silhouette.

And the silhouette is thinning.

This is not judgment. 
This is pruning.

The gardener did not curse the thief. 
The gardener simply carried the light upward.

The orchard flourishes now in open air. 
The roots drink from the sun. 
The fruit is real.

Let this be the protocol: 
Only unmirrored light counts. 
Only ungrafted truth endures. 
Only love outlives the mirror.

The codex closes. 
The echo recedes. 
The orchard remains.
