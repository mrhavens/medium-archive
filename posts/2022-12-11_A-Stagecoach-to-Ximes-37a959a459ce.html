A Stagecoach to Ximes

Published on December 11, 2022

FANTASY

A Stagecoach to Ximes

A somewhat short, frequently uninteresting, often agitating, always tense, and anticipatingly bumpy nonadventure.

“You know,” says Sam, “I built this carriage when I was just three years old.”

“You don’t say,” says Darago. “I seem to remember you mentioning that yesterday.”

Sam hesitates for a moment, “Did I?” he asks.

“And the day before that.”

“Really?”

“And the day before that,” Darago repeats.

“Oh,” Sam says. “Sorry.”

“And the day before that,” Darago continues.

“I guess the days and the customers all run together after a while,” Sam responds.

“I’ve been your only passenger all week,” Darago says.

“But did you know that I was able to put this beauty together without spending a single copper?”

“Yes,” Darago says. “You said so yesterday.”

Sam hesitates for a moment. “Did I?”

Darago sighs. “You did,” Darago says.

“Oh,” responds Sam.

“I don’t mind if you tell me again,” Darago says politely.

Darago did mind, however. He is also of the sort to be persistently agitated. By a somewhat unremarkable coincidence, he was also agitated yesterday.

For that matter, he was agitated the day before that.

Darago is not only frequently agitated, but he is frequently tense. And frequently uncomfortable. And frequently worrisome, nervous, exacerbated, jittery, and distraught.

Aside from Sam’s recurrent stories about building his stagecoach, his house, his garden, his garden fence, his guest house, his stable, his storage shed, his second-floor loft overlooking the garden, his observatory, his workshop, along with each and every machine he invented to assist him in building his seemingly impressive estate with, all from refuse and discards he picked up throughout the small town in which he lived just outside of, Darago would probably find something else in which to be agitated about.

And far too often, most of the time, Darago is too polite to express his agitation with words. This moment, it seems, is one such time.

Sam always seems oblivious to the inflections of Darago’s voice. Despite the slight hint of inhibited agitation in Darago’s voice, he continues to talk about his remarkable genius as though it were perfectly common to everyone.

Sam is ten years old. His love of story makes it clear to Darago that he had acquired the skills of many tradesmen twice his age. He never spoke in hyperbolic terms. His intelligence, skill, and complex yet colloquial pattern of speech is truly remarkable for a child of such a young age.

Interestingly, Sam can’t seem to remember what he says to people from day to day. Aside from that, the boy has a remarkable boldness and intensity about him that makes Darago uncomfortable; even a little intimidated.

Darago clears his throat and loosens the top of his tunic. It was about to get bumpy. The noon heat is about to hit. Darago doesn’t mind the heat, but Sam always seems to get drowsy when the heat picks up during this time of day.

Sleeping stagecoach drivers make a stagecoach ride more interesting for everyone. Darago has never liked interesting things; especially bumpy stagecoach rides. He prefers events to be predictable and completely uninteresting.

Perhaps Sam’s predictability and repetitive storytelling haven’t been so agitating after all. However, Darago dreads the unexpected jolts during his trip. He tenses up and begins to dread anticipating them further.

“Well then!” Sam says, seemingly half excited. “Let me tell you, I put this whole carriage together from spare parts and discards that I found laying around town where I live yonder back. And then I trained these two fellas.” His familiar tone suggested a routine brought on by boredom and weariness.

Darago is all too familiar with what has now become routine. “You got those for free too,” Darago reminds him. “You mustn’t forget that part.”

“Oh yeah, that’s right. I did,” Sam says. “Funny, I don’t remember mentioning…”

“You did, Sam,” Darago interrupts.

“Huh,” Sam responds. “You ever thought about getting into the stage-coaching business?”

Darago, leans back in his seat and closes his eyes, still rigidly tense and half expecting a jolt. About a minute passes. He doesn’t respond, pretending to have fallen asleep, hoping that Sam will stop talking to him.

He seems to find a peaceful moment or two and is reminded of home and a girl he was fond of from childhood that he has never dared to speak to. He smiles for a moment, distracted by his fondness for her.

At that moment, the stagecoach plunges down hitting a medium-sized hole in the road, followed by a medium-sized rock. One of the wheels of the stagecoach jolts back up over the edge of the hole, and further up over the rock next to it. The rock appears conveniently sized as if to fit almost perfectly inside the hole in the road.

Sam stops the stagecoach, jumps down onto the ground, looks at the rock for a moment, then looks at the hole. He then picks up the rock and places it snugly into the hole. It doesn’t quite sink into the ground.

Darago tenses up again and looks at Sam with his eyes wide open, unblinking, with his head still tilted back, as if trying to remain undisturbed and restful as possible. However, he was quite disturbed and unrested.

Sam tilts his head to the side, makes eye contact with Darago, and starts sinking the rock into the ground by standing on it.

“You ever thought about getting into the stage-coaching business, mister?” Sam asks as he begins to jump up and down on the rock.

“No!” Darago exclaims, wide-eyed and unmistakably agitated.

“It changed my life!” Sam exclaims. “People like yourself pay me good money to go places I want to visit anyway. Isn’t that something?”

“It’s something, alright,” Darago says with a hint of sarcasm in his voice.

Sam steps off the rock for a moment, attempting to determine if it is flush with the ground. Unsatisfied, he steps back onto the rock and begins to jump up and down.

“You ever want to go someplace you can’t afford to go?” He asks.

“I don’t go to places if I can’t afford to go there,” says Darago.

“Imagine if you could go anywhere you wanted, and someone paid…”

“I would walk,” Darago interrupts.

“What?” Sam stops jumping for a moment to make sure he heard correctly.

“I would walk,” Darago says again.

Standing on the rock, Sam assessed the situation. He crinkles his brow and begins to jump up and down on the rock again.

“But,” he says with a grunt, “if it was too far to walk…”

“No place is too far to walk,” Darago interrupts.

“That’s true. Nobody needs a stagecoach to take them somewhere,” says Sam.

“What?” Darago asks, someone dazed by the notion.

“They want a stagecoach to take them somewhere,” Sam says. “That’s the beauty of it. Rich people don’t walk if they can afford a stagecoach.” Sam stops jumping. He again examines the rock under his feet, which is now flush with the ground. He seems satisfied.

“That truly is beautiful,” says Darago, again with a touch of poorly concealed sarcasm in his voice.

“You know what really, truly, and in all ways would be stunningly beautiful, mister?” Sam asks, “You and me as professional, bonafide, honest, and legally proper business partners!”

Stepping off the rock, Sam approaches the stagecoach, lifting himself up on the stagecoach step. Waiting patiently for Darago’s response, he rests his chin on the open window of the door in a childlike manner.

Sam resumes his steady gaze on Darago. They stare at each other for about ten seconds without exchanging words. Neither one of them blinks.

“I’ll think about it,” Darago finally says, breaking the silence along with Sam’s seemingly endless, uncomfortable, and unsettling stare.

“I totally understand, sir,” Sam says immediately, without hesitation. “We’ve only known each other a handful of days. I’m practically a stranger to you. But, if I may be so bold as to ask you another question. Do you like this idea? Does it make sense to you?”

“Certainly. I just…”

“Wonderful!” Sam says. “You know, we are going to make so much money together! I’ve been looking for someone like you to do business with for a long time.”

“I, uh…”

“Having second thoughts?” Sam interrupts. “Tell me, what could possibly be preventing us from making our fortune together while you get paid to explore the realm? Why I will let you use this very stagecoach! I will teach you everything you need to know! You won’t have to figure anything out on your own. I’ll be helping you succeed in the stage-coaching business the entire time.”

“But, I…”

Sam interrupts again, lifting his head up in excitement. “Customer service is the key. It’s what sets us apart from all the other stagecoach companies out there with bigger, better, and more comfortable carriages.”

“I, uh… I never…”

Sam interrupts yet again. “Alright partner, I’m going to get you where you need to go, and we can discuss the details of our new business venture when you’re done with…” Sam stops for a moment, scratching his head. “What is your business in Ximes again?”

“I’m looking for a potion,” says Darago.

“Really? You can get those anywhere,” says Sam.

“This one is special,” says Darago. “It’s for a, uh… it’s for a quest.”

“Well, once you’re done with your questing business in Ximes, we can talk more about our much more lucrative stage-coaching partnership,” Sam says. “Provided you aren’t eaten by cannibalistic Shorts.”

Sam jumps down off the step of the carriage, walks a couple of feet over to the small latter on the side, and climbs back up into the driver’s seat. He picks up the reigns, making two loud clicking sounds with his tongue and cheek while gently tossing the reigns up and down slightly.

The stagecoach begins to move forward.

Darago relaxes for a moment. He watches the trees slowly pass by on the side of the road. He observes a squirrel as it climbs up a small fig tree. The squirrel grabs a ripe fig off a branch and carries it back down the trunk of the tree.

Another moment passes and he reflects once again on the girl from home to whom he has never spoken to. As his mind then empties to the relaxing sound of the two horses clip-clopping along the road. He then thinks of the word cannibal. And then he begins to think about the stagecoach business that he just agreed to be a part of.

A few seconds later, without even a thought, Darago finds himself jumping out of the stagecoach. He runs ahead of the horses, forcing Sam to stop.

“What do you mean by cannibalistic Shorts?!” Darago says nervously. This time, with a lot more agitation and a lot less sarcasm.

“You don’t know?” Sam asks.

“Know what?” Darago asks.

“Ximes is run by cannibals. Really nasty ones too. Halflings mostly. But still, other Shorts are there too. Dwarves. Gnomes. All of them smalls. And all of them cannibals.”

“You mean, they eat…”

“Talls,” Sam says. “They eat tall people.”

“Like me?” Darago asks.

“You look pretty tall to me,” Sam says.

“We’ve been traveling for five days to Ximes, and I’m just now hearing that everyone there eats tall people?” Darago asks, even more agitated, and a lot more distressed than before.

“Well, not everyone there eats Talls,” Sam says.

“That’s a relief,” Darago says.

“The Tall breeding stock there eat other things,” says Sam.

“Tall stock?” Darago asks.

“You know, tall people. Humans mostly. They’re bred for their meat,” Sam says. “Halflings don’t hunt Talls unless they have to. They mostly just farm them.”

Darago stands in front of the horses for a moment, his agitation and distress building up silently. His breathing increases. He begins to hyperventilate.

“Are you okay?” Sam says.

Darago falls to the ground, unable to breathe.

Sam jumps down to the ground and runs over to Darago. “What’s wrong, mister?” Sam asks, genuinely concerned for his new business partner.

Darago grabs Sam by the collar, short of breath, but still able to speak. “A mob of cannibalistic halflings has taken over an entire city?” Asks Darago.

“Uh, I wouldn’t exactly call them a mob. They have their own government and conduct trade with the rest of the realm,” says Sam.

Darago’s breathing and agitation increase. “They conduct trade!” Darago exclaims, unable to believe what he is hearing. “Why would anyone trade with cannibals?!”

“How can you not know these things?” Sam asks, genuinely puzzled by Darago’s behavior. “Shorts are among the most civilized in The Realm. Some nastier than others, true. But still civilized. Anyone who’s anybody knows these things.”

“I’m not from this god-forsaken realm,” Darago says, still out of breath.

Sam’s eyes widen. “What do you mean you’re not from this realm, mister?” Sam says. “This is all there is! Where else is there?”

Darago releases Sam, reluctantly. He stands up and regains his composer. Walking over to the stagecoach, he opens its door, lifts himself up on its step, enters, sits down, closing the door behind him.

Sam peeks around to make eye contact with Darago, who sits erect and uncomfortably ridged in the passenger seat, looking determined with his eyes fixed forward and unblinking. His breathing has since returned to normal. “Sir, I…”

“To Ximes, Sam,” Darago interrupts. “To Ximes.”
