Unlock the Hero Within: Understanding the Power of The Hero’s Journey

Published on November 03, 2023 at 12:02 PM

FICTION WRITING

Unlock the Hero Within: Understanding the Power of The Hero’s Journey

A guide to inspiring and crafting epic fantasy adventures

Introduction

Life has no meaning. Each of us has meaning and we bring it to life. It is a waste to be asking the question when you are the answer.
― Joseph Campbell

It is our responsibility as writers to create our own meaning, bring it to life, and then share that meaning with our readers.

I have learned the value of the “hero’s journey” as a way to overcome creative challenges. This idea, defined by Joseph Campbell, is a cycle of growth, adventure, and self-discovery.

As a fantasy and science fiction writer, I have often found myself facing difficult challenges and obstacles that have tested my creativity and commitment to the craft. I have come to understand the importance of embracing the “hero’s journey” — a concept that has been defined by Joseph Campbell as a universal cycle of growth, adventure, and self-discovery — as a way of overcoming these obstacles.

My understanding of the hero’s journey has enabled me to gain the confidence and courage necessary to move forward, even in the face of adversity. As a result, I have unlocked a new level of creativity and potential.

The Hero’s Journey was not created by any one individual but rather was identified by Joseph Campbell, who was a noted scholar of comparative mythology. He observed and studied the stories of countless cultures throughout history and discovered the common patterns that connected them. This pattern, which is now known as the Hero’s Journey, represents the journey that we all take throughout our lives, no matter our background or culture.

By providing me with a framework proven to work within the human condition, this timeless story structure has helped me become a better storyteller. Through understanding The Hero’s Journey, I have been able to craft stories with clear objectives, arcs, and character development, allowing me to create more relatable characters within the worlds I construct.

With The Hero’s Journey, I can create stories that are emotionally engaging and emotionally moving, allowing me to explore new depths of storytelling.

Having studied Campbell’s work, I now find inspiration in ancient myths and stories of our ancestors.

I’ve always been a fan of the Lord of the Rings and Dungeons & Dragons since I was a child. I have now begun to deep dive into classic mythology with newfound respect. After all, The Hero’s Journey is a timeless framework that has been used to craft meaningful stories used in our mythology for thousands of years.

Maybe, with a little luck and some serious editing, my stories too will be read a few thousand years from now.

ACT I: Departure

People say that what we’re all seeking is a meaning for life. I don’t think that’s what we’re really seeking. I think that what we’re seeking is an experience of being alive, so that our life experiences on the purely physical plane will have resonances with our own innermost being and reality, so that we actually feel the rapture of being alive.
― Joseph Campbell, The Power of Myth

This quote from Joseph Campbell’s The Power of Myth speaks to the idea that life is not about finding a specific meaning or purpose, but rather about experiencing the joy and rapture of being alive. This is an essential concept in the Hero’s Journey, which is a common motif in literature.

The Hero’s Journey is a narrative structure that follows the journey of a hero as they embark on a quest to overcome obstacles and gain knowledge and power. The hero begins in the ordinary world, then is called to adventure, faces challenges and tests, and eventually returns transformed.

At each step of the Hero’s Journey, the hero must confront their fears and doubts, and grapple with the meaning of life. In this way, the Hero’s Journey is a metaphor for the inner journey of self-discovery.

The hero must learn to embrace being alive, revel in the experience of life, and find meaning in the journey itself.

This — the ultimate goal of The Hero’s Journey — is to find a sense of purpose and meaning in life, not only for the hero but for all of us. The quote speaks to the idea that we should not be seeking a specific meaning or purpose, but rather enjoy being alive and find joy and rapture in the journey.

Ultimately, Campbell speaks to the idea that life is not about searching for some predetermined meaning or purpose, but rather that we should embrace the unknown and find joy in the experiences of life.

The Hero’s Journey is a powerful metaphor for this idea and serves to remind us that the journey of life is not only full of obstacles and tests but also full of moments of beauty and joy that can be found if we take the time to search for them.

It is during the Departure Phase of the Hero’s Journey that individuals must push themselves beyond their comfort zones in order to find their true purpose. In Campbell’s view, life isn’t simply about finding meaning, but also about experiencing the joy of living.

A story’s departure is an invitation to embrace the unknown and explore the depths of our innermost selves.

The Call to Adventure

A hero is someone who has given his or her life to something bigger than oneself.
— Joseph Campbell, The Hero with a Thousand Faces

The Call to Adventure phase of the Hero’s Journey is a time of transition when a hero must make the difficult decision to leave the comfort and security of their ordinary world to embark on a quest that will test their courage and strength of character.

This call to a greater purpose requires courage, self-sacrifice, and a willingness to put something larger than oneself first.

Campbell reminds us that true heroism is found in this commitment to something bigger and more meaningful than our own individual interests.

The Hero’s Journey usually starts in their normal life, where they are able to receive a call for adventure. The call might take them to a faraway land, a mysterious jungle, a kingdom below the waves or above the sky, an island hidden from view, a high mountaintop, or even a dream state.

These places can contain bizarre beings, unimaginable tortures, superhuman acts, and impossible delights.

The hero may choose to take the journey. Theseus did so after hearing the tale of the Minotaur. Perhaps you may send your character on a journey like Odysseus, who was driven by Poseidon’s wind around the Mediterranean.

It can be started wilfully, by accident, or by force. Or perhaps it can be simple curiosity. Something that catches the hero’s attention and leads them down a different path.

Refusal of the Call

We must be willing to let go of the life we planned so as to have the life that is waiting for us.
― Joseph Campbell, The Power of Myth

Our hero’s destiny has laid out a challenge for them, but they are reluctant to accept it.

In this phase, a hero must set aside their own plans and desires in order to pursue their higher purpose. It is a crucial step on the journey toward self-actualization and requires strength and courage to overcome fear.

A future hero may refuse to answer a call from the future for fear, doubt, or a sense of duty. This can lead to a negative adventure where the protagonist becomes stuck in boredom, hard labor, or a rigid culture. Their life becomes meaningless and the world they live in becomes a desert. Even if they can build an impressive empire it will still be a house full of death. They will die one day. When creating fantasy stories, aspiring writers need to keep this in mind.

Supernatural Aid

It may be a species of impudence to think that the way you understand God is the way God is.
— Joseph Campbell

Campbell reminds us that it is impudent to assume that our understanding of God is the only way to perceive the divine. This is particularly relevant to the Supernatural Aid phase of the Hero’s Journey, which calls upon the Hero to accept the assistance of the supernatural that is a manifestation of their destiny.

It is in this phase that the Hero must learn to accept assistance from something that they don’t fully understand, and thus, must be willing to trust in the guidance of an unknown source of power.

The Supernatural Aid phase of the Hero’s Journey is a pivotal moment in the transformation of the hero. It is a stage that marks the beginning of the hero’s quest to discover the true nature of their destiny.

During this stage, the hero is confronted with a mysterious figure of supernatural power, who provides them with the objects and talismans they need to complete their journey. This figure is symbolic of the protective power of the mother’s womb and serves as a reminder to the hero that nature will be there to support them in their quest, no matter how powerless or helpless they may feel.

The Supernatural Aid phase thus acts as a crucial reminder of the hero’s alignment with the actions of society, and the great beat of the historical process.

The Crossing of the First Threshold

The cave you fear to enter holds the treasure you seek.
― Joseph Campbell, The Hero with a Thousand Faces

This step is the point of no return, a moment that marks the beginning of a hero’s great adventure. It is often a difficult, confronting, and often frightening experience that requires an immense amount of courage and determination.

It is a test of a hero’s mettle, to see if they can overcome their fears and doubts and take the risk of entering an unknown space. It is an essential moment in the journey, as it marks the start of the hero’s transformation and growth. Campbell reminds us that the journey is a necessary part of life and that, in order to gain the rewards of the journey, one must be willing to take the first step and cross the first threshold.

It is an important step in the hero’s quest. It is when they move out of their safe, familiar world into unfamiliar and risky territory. A threshold guardian could mark this transition — a person who protects them from the dangers of their current lives. This is dangerous because it opens up the unknown. Normal people tend to be content with staying within their comfort zone. But, for experienced adventurers who are brave and capable, the risk is worth it. It’s an adventure from the familiar to the unknown that requires crossing the threshold. This is the challenge for every fantasy writer.

Belly of the Whale

We must let go of the life we have planned, so as to accept the one that is waiting for us.
— Joseph Campbell

Here, the hero embarks on a metaphorical journey of self-discovery and transformation, in which they must confront their fears and accept the unknown in order to move forward. The Belly of the Whale serves as a reminder that, although it can be difficult to let go of our preconceived notions, embracing the unknown can lead to a more rewarding and fulfilling journey.

This is a symbol of a character’s journey to new places. This stage requires that the character is willing to go through a transformation.

This stage is often used as a way to express the concept of self-sacrifice. It is believed that the character dies in the same world as they knew, but is reborn in another form. This is often depicted in stories with the hero falling through a hole or being swallowed up by a whale.

This is illustrated in the Book of Jonah. Jonah tries to escape God’s command to destroy Nineveh, but instead is thrown overboard by a storm and swallowed in a “great fish.” Jonah obeys God’s will over three days and is then safely dumped onto a beach. He then returns to Nineveh, preaching to its people.

Jonah’s journey through a belly of a whale is symbolic of death and resurrection and is frequently used in Jungian analysis. When crafting fantasy stories, it’s an important symbol to consider.

ACT II: Initiation

When we quit thinking primarily of ourselves and our own self-preservation, we undergo a truly heroic transformation of consciousness.
— Joseph Campbell, The Hero with a Thousand Faces

The initiation phase of the Hero’s Journey is a transformative process that requires courage and selflessness. As Joseph Campbell so eloquently states, the true transformation of consciousness occurs when we move away from self-preservation and begin to think of others. It is during this stage of the Hero’s Journey that we are challenged to face our fears and take risks in order to grow and become a better version of ourselves.

The Road of Trials

I always tell my students, go where your body and soul want to go. When you have the feeling, then stay with it, and don’t let anyone throw you off.
— Joseph Campbell

The Road of Trials is an essential part of the Hero’s Journey, as it tests the hero’s courage and strength of character.

This is a time when the hero must overcome obstacles, face difficult challenges and make difficult decisions. It is essential for the hero to remain focused on their mission and to stay the course no matter what obstacles may come their way.

There are many writers who use the road of trials to introduce the transformation of their hero. The tests are typically presented in three stages and your hero must pass these stages. Even though your hero may fail, they should eventually overcome each challenge.

Their journey may even take them into an alternate state, perhaps a dream world with strange and ambiguous forms.

This stage may be assisted by secret agents, supernatural entities, or mysterious artifacts.

These moments are filled with joy, bliss, and glimpses of a magical world.

To reach the end, the hero must keep moving forward and avoid danger.

The Meeting with the Goddess

Both the artist and the lover know that perfection is not loveable. It is the clumsiness of a fault that makes a person lovable.
— Joseph Campbell

In life, it is often our imperfections that make us the most lovable. This is particularly true in The Meeting with the Goddess phase, which involves a meeting with an otherworldly being that represents the ultimate source of divine love.

It is through the recognition of our flaws and vulnerabilities that we can truly experience the depths of love and connection. This is an essential part of the Hero’s Journey and is integral to the transformation of the hero from ordinary to extraordinary.

Writers should also be aware of the practical significance that this meeting has. It is where the hero gets items that will assist them on their journey.

Often depicted as a magical union between the conquering hero and a powerful goddess, this is the character’s personal climax of their internal story. It’s the final test of a hero’s ability to win ultimate love.

If a female character is involved, she will be chosen to be an immortal’s consort for her beauty, charm, or yearning. Even if he has tried to stop her, she will see the light in his eyes and will become more open to her influence. If she has sought him out, her longings for him will become fulfilled.

Woman as the Temptress

If marriage isn’t a first priority in your life, you’re not married.
— Joseph Campbell

In this phase, the hero must confront their desires and decide which path to take. This quote speaks directly to the temptress, who can often lead the hero astray if he does not prioritize his destiny over his own desires.

A hero must demonstrate that they have the strength of character and commitment to make the right decision to continue on the path of greatness.

In stories, a woman is often portrayed as a symbol of the temptations that can distract a hero-knight. These temptations must be overcome by the hero for the hero to succeed. While striving to attain a higher, more spiritual realm, they must acknowledge and accept that the “odor of flesh” is a part of human life.

Atonement with the Father

It’s only when a man tames his own demons that he becomes the king of himself, if not of the world.
— Joseph Campbell

Atonement with the Father is an essential part of the Hero’s Journey and is often a daunting challenge. This is especially true when it comes to the atonement with the father-god, which is a powerful reflection of the psychological rivalry and subconscious hatred that Freudian psychoanalysts believe so many children (particularly young men) feel toward their fathers.

The Hero’s Journey is an archetypal story of overcoming adversity and confronting the inner demons that stand in the way of true liberation and power.

The hero must face and be initiated by the ultimate source of power in their lives, which is often represented by a father or father figure. Although it can be difficult at times, it is an essential part in order to keep the story moving forward. The hero should have faith in the Father’s mercy and be prepared to depend on the support of the mother figure or other female figure for support.

This stage ends with the hero transcending life and seeing the source. They realize the majestic truths of existence and achieve harmony, achieving atonement. At this point, the character can begin the next stage of their journey.

Apotheosis

Those who know, not only that the Everlasting lies in them, but that what they, and all things, really are is the Everlasting, dwell in the groves of the wish-fulfilling trees, drink the brew of immortality, and listen everywhere to the unheard music of eternal concord.
— Joseph Campbell

Campbell speaks of a greater understanding of one’s true self, achieved through self-reflection and inner transformation. This idea of achieving a higher place through self-realization is at the heart of the Apotheosis phase of the Hero’s Journey.

This phase is a moment in which the hero transcends, growing beyond the trials and tribulations of the preceding stages to reach a state of divine knowledge, love, compassion, and bliss.

Through this transformation, the hero is reborn, ready to face the ultimate challenge with glory and joy.

The moment a hero gains a deeper understanding of the adventure that they are embarking on, their knowledge and insight give them the ability and courage to face more difficult challenges.

The Ultimate Boon

The goal of life is to make your heartbeat match the beat of the universe, to match your nature with Nature.
— Joseph Campbell

The Ultimate Boon phase of the Hero’s Journey is the ultimate goal of life for many, as it embodies the idea that one can reach the highest level of fulfillment and connection with the universe.

Through the hero’s journey, we can understand that the Ultimate Boon is not necessarily a physical object or reward, but rather a spiritual and emotional understanding of one’s place in the universe, from which power is derived. It is a journey of self-discovery and personal growth, culminating in an experience of oneness with the universe.

Ultimately, the hero is seeking not just the grace of the gods, but their sustaining substance — the Imperishable — which can only be released to those who have proven themselves worthy.

As an ultimate reward for a hero who has completed their journey towards worthiness or enlightenment, this stage is symbolic of the hero’s transformation into a being of greater power and insight and can be represented by supernatural forces.

The hero might be rewarded with endless wealth, immortality, or the formidable power of the gods, which will serve to help them reach their ultimate goal.

ACT III: Return

The principle of compassion is that which converts disillusionment into a participatory companionship. This is the basic love, the charity, that turns a critic into a living human being who has something to give to — as well as to demand of — the world.
— Joseph Campbell

The Return phase of the Hero’s Journey is a stage of transformation, where the hero is able to bring back a newfound understanding of the world. This newfound understanding is often fueled by compassion for those left behind and is an essential element of the hero’s journey. It enables the hero to look at the world with a newfound perspective, understanding, and appreciation.

Refusal of the Return

Find a place inside where there’s joy, and the joy will burn out the pain.
― Joseph Campbell

Our protagonist must choose between staying in a place of bliss and enlightenment or returning home after achieving their goal. The reluctance to return home is a test of the hero’s selflessness, as they must choose between their own desires and the greater good. The hero must decide if they will accept the ultimate boon and share it with the world, or if they will keep it all to themselves.

It is a test of courage, a test of commitment, and a test of loyalty.

The Refusal of the Return is a powerful moment in the Hero’s Journey and a testament to the strength of the protagonist’s character.

The Magic Flight

Follow your bliss and the universe will open doors for you where there were only walls.
― Joseph Campbell

The Magic Flight of the hero’s journey is a stage where the hero must face seemingly insurmountable obstacles in order to return home with the elixir they have stolen from the gods. Writers should demonstrate the courage and strength of will it takes for their characters to endure this stage and successfully return home. This stage is also a symbolic representation of the courage and resilience that we all must possess in order to overcome the obstacles that life throws at us.

This element is a critical and often perilous transition from the realm of the supernatural back to the everyday world, where the hero must face the consequences of his or her actions. It is a moment when the gods, demons, and supernatural forces can be in pursuit of the hero, either to reclaim the elixir that has been taken, to prevent the hero from taking the ultimate boon, or to rescue the hero from harm.

This phase of our character’s journey is filled with danger, excitement, and suspense, and the hero must rely on their wit and skills to make their way back home.

The way your protagonist escapes with the boon may be just as thrilling and exciting as the journey to get it in your story. When they return with something that restores society with the blessing of an angel or goddess, they can count on the support of their patron supernatural. When the hero is unable to return an item or obtains it against the guardian’s wishes, he may face dangerous pursuits.

Magical obstacles and evasion techniques can make the adventure even more exciting.

Rescue from Without

Whatever the hell happens, say, ‘This is what I need.’
— Joseph Campbell, The Power of Myth

No hero can complete their journey alone, and the power of the external benefactor is an essential element in the Rescue from Without stage of the hero’s journey. It is in this phase that the hero must rely on forces beyond themselves to make it back home, and it is only through the assistance of these powerful guides that the hero can be saved.

Despite their power, characters should still be vulnerable and willing to accept help from others, regardless of their level of power. During the rescue from without phase of the hero’s journey, the external benefactor plays an important role in recognizing the need for help when returning from the supernatural realm.

The hero may need to be physically rescued themselves, or they may need guidance on how to return home and share the boon they have won.

The hero may have to be rescued by someone else, perhaps because he has been captured, or because he or she cannot leave their hiding place. As a result of the boon, the hero may not be able to return for a while. It is always possible to try to rescue in any case, and those that try to do so may be surprised if the hero is unwilling to return.

The Crossing of the Return Threshold

The adventure of the hero is to survive all the obstacles of initiation and move on to what those obstacles have made possible — which is a deeper understanding of what life is.
— Joseph Campbell, The Hero with a Thousand Faces

The Crossing of the Return Threshold is a pivotal and essential point in the Hero’s Journey, as it marks the hero’s return from the adventure with newfound wisdom and strength. To learn more about what life is, a hero must overcome obstacles in order to gain a deeper understanding of it. In order to move forward in their journey, the hero must survive the initiation and use the knowledge gained to do so.

The hero must be open to the challenges of initiation and strive to use the wisdom gained from their journey to improve their lives and the lives of those around them. The hero must recognize that their journey may be misunderstood by others, but must have faith in the spiritual truths they have uncovered and use them to create a better world.

The hero’s ultimate goal is to gain insight and understanding into life.

Master of the Two Worlds

Half the people in the world think that the metaphors of their religious traditions, for example, are facts. And the other half contends that they are not facts at all. As a result we have people who consider themselves believers because they accept metaphors as facts, and we have others who classify themselves as atheists because they think religious metaphors are lies.
― Joseph Campbell, Thou Art That: Transforming Religious Metaphor

Campbell highlights the apparent dichotomy between believers and non-believers and the power of religious metaphors. This dichotomy is a major component during this phase of the Hero’s Journey.

The Hero must reconcile the seemingly opposing forces of the spiritual and physical worlds, learning to navigate and understand both in order to move forward. The Hero must understand that the two worlds are not mutually exclusive and can exist in harmony; it is this balance between the two that allows the Hero to ultimately become a Master of the Two Worlds.

The hero can therefore move freely between the inner and outer worlds. Without having to confuse the principles of each, they are able to understand both one and the other. This ability requires mental discipline and the willingness to give up personal ambitions and attachments in order to rebirth in the realization and fulfillment of truth.

Freedom to Live

If you can see your path laid out in front of you step by step, you know it’s not your path. Your own path you make with every step you take. That’s why it’s your path.
― Joseph Campbell

This is a crucial stage where the hero finally sets out on his journey towards a better life, liberated from the shackles of their former self. It is a time of self-discovery, where the hero embarks on a path of their own making, guided by the newfound courage and strength to face the unknown.

The hero is someone who has not yet established a legacy but is a champion of change. This is because they know that nothing really dies. Instead, it changes form. So they don’t fear what the future holds, they embrace it and all its possibilities.

Nothing retains its own form; but Nature, the greater renewer, ever makes up forms from forms.
— Ovid, a Roman poet

This phase in the Hero’s Journey is a time of renewal when the hero has overcome their struggles and is ready to embrace a new life. Ovid’s quote speaks to this idea, suggesting that nothing stays the same, but that Nature is constantly transforming and renewing forms.

Nature provides an opportunity for the hero to start anew, to create a new identity and a new life, one that is more in harmony with their true nature. Just as Nature is always in a state of flux, the hero must also accept and embrace change in order to live a life of freedom and fulfillment.

With this understanding, the hero can live freely without fear of death and in the present.

Conclusion

Life is like arriving late for a movie, having to figure out what was going on without bothering everyone with a lot of questions, and then being unexpectedly called away before you find out how it ends.
— Joseph Campbell, The Masks of God, Volume 4

Like the hero, we may not understand the full picture of our lives until we reach the end.

Writers can use the Hero’s Journey to craft a narrative that captures this sense of discovery and exploration. By following the steps of the journey, writers can capture the arc of a hero’s life and create a story that is both meaningful and captivating.

In the end, the hero’s journey can be a way of finding the answers to life’s great questions and discovering the resolution of our story.

As you proceed through life, following your own path, birds will shit on you. Don’t bother to brush it off. Getting a comedic view of your situation gives you spiritual distance. Having a sense of humor saves you.
— Joseph Campbell

The Hero’s Journey is ultimately a journey of self-discovery and growth, and Campbell speaks to this idea in a unique and humorous way. His words remind us that life will always be filled with challenges, and it is our ability to take these experiences in stride that helps us stay on our path and reach our ultimate destination.

With a comedic view of our situation, we are able to take a step back and gain spiritual distance, which ultimately allows us to gain the strength and courage needed to continue on our quest.
