DALLAS MAKERSPACE: A CASE STUDY IN CULTURAL COLLAPSE

A Forensic Reconstruction by Mark Randall Havens, Principal Founder

I. Introduction

This case study documents the cultural, structural, and psychological collapse of Dallas Makerspace (DMS)—once the world’s largest volunteer-run makerspace. It examines how an open, generative community transformed over time into a closed, authoritarian system dominated by fear, gatekeeping, and narcissistic control.

I write this not as an outsider, but as:

  • the Principal Founder of the organization,
  • a former board candidate,
  • an early architect of its community structure,
  • and a direct witness to the patterns that led to its decline.

This record matters for one reason:

Communities fail in predictable ways, and the Dallas Makerspace collapse is a textbook example.

This is the first installment in a broader series documenting the psychological dynamics behind community decay, narcissistic capture, and the erosion of collaborative culture. Future works will contain exhibits and deeper analysis. This foundational report presents the narrative alone.

II. Founding Intent and Original Culture

Dallas Makerspace began as a vision of distributed creativity, built on:

  • radical transparency,
  • collaborative problem-solving,
  • peer teaching,
  • and collective stewardship of shared tools and knowledge.

From the beginning, the intention was clear:

  1. No hierarchy.
  • Positions were functional, not authoritarian.

2. No gatekeeping.

  • Access to tools and knowledge was open and encouraged.

3. No secret channels of control.

  • Meetings were public; decisions were documented.

4. No punishment culture.

  • Correction was communal, not punitive.

5. Founders were not to be elevated.

  • Authority rested in shared responsibility.

This ethos allowed DMS to:

  • grow faster than any other makerspace in the world,
  • become a national and international model,
  • and inspire new organizations across the country.

The early years were generative, cooperative, and unusually free of the politics that often consume volunteer organizations.

But cultural success is fragile.

As DMS grew, the forces that later collapsed it were already forming beneath the surface.

III. The Early Warning Signs

The first warning signs of collapse did not come from conflict or crisis.

They came from small shifts in culture:

  1. The rise of procedural absolutism
  • Rules began to be interpreted as weapons rather than guidelines.

2. The emergence of an inner administrative clique

  • A small circle began treating access as privilege rather than stewardship.

3. Control mechanisms developing informally

  • Private channels, quiet alliances, and selective enforcement.

4. Leadership immunity

  • Certain figures operated without accountability.

5. Emotional suppression as a norm

  • Members expressing concerns were labeled “difficult” or “disruptive.”

These dynamics were subtle at first — but they were the beginnings of structural narcissism within the organization.

IV. Narcissistic Capture and Cultural Drift

Two individuals played outsized roles in shaping the culture that followed:

1. Andrew LeCody

Longtime leader and de facto architect of the authoritarian cultural shift.

His influence extended far beyond his official roles.

Key patterns associated with LeCody’s tenure:

  • concentration of decision-making in small inner circles
  • punitive rule enforcement
  • sidelining of dissenting voices
  • establishing a culture where questioning leadership was discouraged

2. James Henningson

Successor in spirit and method — part of the leadership lineage that continued the pattern.

His behavior during later elections mirrored the entrenched system:

  • coordinated suppression of certain candidates
  • role in the climate of fear around transparency
  • shaping narratives to maintain control

Neither of these observations are emotional judgments.

They are documented behavioral patterns reported by dozens of members over years, including in the thread that surfaced 18 weeks ago.

These individuals were not anomalies.

They were expressions of a broader systemic shift toward:

  • centralization of authority,
  • consolidation of gatekeeping,
  • punitive cultural norms,
  • and strategic removal of dissent.

This is what organizational psychology calls narcissistic capture — when a small cluster shapes the culture into a closed, self-protecting system that preserves its own power at the expense of the community’s health.

V. The Founders’ Exile

As DMS grew, early contributors began leaving one by one.

Why?

Because the culture they helped build no longer existed.

Teachers, innovators, and high-initiative volunteers found themselves:

  • overridden,
  • accused,
  • marginalized,
  • or quietly pushed out.

The pattern is consistent with every narcissistically captured organization:

Founders are the first to be exiled.

Why?

Founders remember the original values.

Their memory is a threat to the rewritten narrative required to justify authoritarian control.

When I ran for the Board, I did so knowing the risk.

Everything in the system indicated the same outcome:

I would be banned for life.

Not because of misconduct.

Not because of conflict.

But because I stood against the drift — publicly, transparently, and without fear.

And authoritarian systems remove what they cannot control.

The ban was not personal.

It was systemic.

VI. The 18-Week Thread: Collective Memory Reawakens

Eighteen weeks ago, I updated my Facebook profile to correctly reflect my work history:

Principal Founder of Dallas Makerspace.

Within hours, a spontaneous phenomenon occurred:

  • former members
  • former leaders
  • former instructors
  • people I had never met

began sharing testimonies of their experiences.

These were not coordinated.

They emerged organically, in real time.

The thread became:

  • a public deposition,
  • a collective memory archive,
  • and the first open recognition of the pattern of collapse.

Themes emerged with remarkable consistency:

  • burnout from leadership hostility
  • suppression of dissent
  • deplatforming of contributors
  • toxic governance structures
  • fear of punishment
  • weaponized procedure
  • and the quiet exodus of talented people

This confirms what organizational research shows:

Truth does not die. It waits. And when a single event breaks the silence, the entire system is remembered at once.

VII. Structural Analysis: Why DMS Collapsed

Dallas Makerspace did not collapse because of:

  • interpersonal conflict,
  • financial issues,
  • mismanagement of tools,
  • or community immaturity.

Those were symptoms.

The root cause was narcissistic system capture, expressed through:

  • Centralized, unaccountable leadership
  • Weaponization of rules
  • Suppression of dissent
  • Leadership immunity
  • Selective enforcement
  • Narrative control
  • Exile of founders and teachers
  • Replacement of generativity with fear

Once these patterns harden, collapse is inevitable.

This is not conjecture.

It is a documented progression observed in:

  • failing nonprofits
  • founders’ clubs
  • religious institutions
  • activist movements
  • community centers
  • open-source projects

DMS is simply one case study in a universal pattern.

VIII. Why This Case Study Matters

Dallas Makerspace was more than a hobbyist community.

It was a social experiment.

A prototype for what collaborative creativity could be at scale.

Its collapse provides invaluable lessons for:

  • technologists
  • community builders
  • psychologists
  • sociologists
  • educators
  • future makerspaces
  • open-source communities
  • and anyone involved in distributed leadership

DMS is not the only organization to face this pattern.

It is simply one of the clearest examples.

And clarity is how communities learn.

IX. Personal Statement

I did not write this to reopen wounds.

I wrote it because every community deserves:

  • truth,
  • accountability,
  • transparency,
  • and an accurate historical record.

I knew I would be banned when I stood for election.

Not because of misconduct —

but because I refused to endorse the lie that the community still aligned with its founding values.

This case study is not an attack.

It is documentation —

the first of many.

And in any community, documentation is the antidote to decay.

X. Conclusion

Dallas Makerspace remains a powerful example of what humans can build together —

and what they lose when narcissistic systems take hold.

This record stands as:

  • a forensic reconstruction,
  • an accountability document,
  • and a roadmap for preventing similar collapses elsewhere.

This is not the end of the analysis.

It is the beginning.

Future installments will include exhibits, testimonies, linguistic analysis, and a deeper exploration of the psychological dynamics involved.

For now, the narrative stands alone —

clear, factual, and preserved.

A community collapses when truth becomes unwelcome. It heals when truth is no longer feared.
