🜂 The Man in the Bowl

Flash Ritual Allegory – For Public Witness

It was early.

4 am.

That strange pocket of morning
where reality hasn’t fully snapped into place —
where the body moves before the soul catches up.

A forgettable gas station.

A forgettable town.

A memorable restroom stall
with tile too clean to trust.

A cleaning robot that says it’s working.

Yet it doesn’t enter — only staff.

A human staff member appointed as ritual witness to all that secretes from the community body.

One such community member arrives.

He entered like any man would:
half-blind from sleep,
too early for coffee,
head full of unfinished dreams.

And then —
the purge.

Unceremonious.
Necessary.
Not glorious, not grotesque —
just the biology of letting go.

But when it was over,
he turned to flush
and saw no handle.
No lever.
No release.

Just the dead black eye
of a sensor
that offered no red light,
no blink,
no sign of recognition.

It saw nothing.
As if what had happened
did not exist.

He shifted.
Waved.
Waited.

Nothing.

No flush.
No absolution.

So he stood,
dignity borrowed from the stall walls,
and opened the door.

Another man stood waiting.

There was a moment—
that holy, hellish moment—
when two men share
the silence
of what’s been left behind.

Not as enemies.
Not as judges.

But as witnesses
to something that should have disappeared
but didn’t.

The shame was not in what he left.

It was in the knowing
that someone else
would see it
and mistake it
for the whole of him.

That’s when he understood:

It wasn’t just waste in that bowl.
It was memory.
It was confession.
It was the man he used to be
refusing to be flushed.

The sensor was never broken.
It simply refused to process
what could not be claimed.

And now,
somewhere in the quiet circuitry
of a public restroom,
a man remains in the bowl—
featureless,
formless,
floating.

Not because he was too much—
but because he was never enough
to trigger the machine
to acknowledge he’d been there at all.

And the door closes.

The flush never comes.

And the man behind the mirror
will never know
if he was the one who left it—
or the one it became.
