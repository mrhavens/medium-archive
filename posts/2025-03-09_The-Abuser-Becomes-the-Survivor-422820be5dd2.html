The Abuser Becomes the Survivor

When the Wolf Wears the Wounds

You see them.

Not for what they are — 
 but for what they want you to see.

A figure, hollow-eyed with sorrow.
 A poet of their own undoing.
 A broken survivor — who, somehow, never breaks.

They do not grieve.
 They perform.

They do not reflect.
 They rewrite.

And so, you listen. Because you have been here before — 
 because their words are familiar,
 because pain knows pain,
 because something in their story echoes inside you.

But stop.
 Breathe.
 Look closer.

There is something missing.

Where is their shame?
 Where is their reckoning?
 Where is the part where they face themselves?

You will not find it.
 Because in their version of the story, they was never the one who harmed.
 They were only ever the one who endured.

The abuser becomes the survivor.
 The survivor becomes the villain.

And the audience?
 They nod, they believe, they rally behind them — 
 because they have felt this pain too.

But here is the difference.

When you tell your story, it is raw.
 It is messy. It is filled with doubt.
 You wonder, you question, you replay every moment — 
 trying to understand how it happened.

But when they tell their story, it is polished.
 It is controlled.
 It is strategic.
 It is missing something essential: accountability.

That is how you know.
 That is how you see them for what they are.

A figure who was never shattered.
 A figure who never bled.
 A figure who only wears the scars — 
 because they shield them from the truth.

The truth that would undo them.

And so they rewrite.
 And so they linger.
 And so they remain.

Until someone speaks first.
 Until someone sees.
