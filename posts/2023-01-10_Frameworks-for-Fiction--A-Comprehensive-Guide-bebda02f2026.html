Frameworks for Fiction: A Comprehensive Guide

Unlocking the secrets of storytelling

Are you struggling to bring your story to life? Look no further! The following is an index of the tried and true methods used by bestselling authors and storytellers to create captivating narratives.

From the Snowflake Method to the Trivia-Mystery structure, this list covers a wide range of techniques and tools that will help you unlock the secrets of storytelling.

Whether you’re a seasoned writer looking to take your craft to the next level, or a beginner just starting out, this comprehensive resource can serve as an invaluable guide for anyone looking to improve their fiction writing skills.

Dive in and discover the frameworks that will help you bring your story to life and captivate your audience.

  1. The 22 Steps — This method, created by author and writing coach Dan Wells, is a step-by-step process for creating a story that is both emotionally engaging and structurally sound. It consists of 22 steps, which are divided into three main stages: the beginning, the middle, and the end.
  2. The 36 Dramatic Situations — This framework, created by Georges Polti, is based on the idea that all stories can be broken down into 36 basic dramatic situations. Each situation corresponds to a specific type of conflict and can be used as a guide for developing a story’s plot and characters.
  3. The Absurdist structure — This framework is used to tell a story that defies logic and reason, often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of comedy.
  4. The Allegory structure — This framework is used to tell a story with a symbolic meaning, it can be used to explore different themes and to create a sense of depth.
  5. The Anti-Hero structure — This framework is used to tell a story of a protagonist who lacks traditional heroic qualities, often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of realism.
  6. The Beat Sheet — The Beat Sheet is a popular screenwriting framework created by Blake Snyder that helps the writer map the story’s beats, it consists of 15 beats that the story should hit, and it helps the writer to stay focused on the plot of the story, and it’s widely used in screenwriting to structure the story.
  7. The Bildungsroman structure — This framework is used to tell a story of a character’s formative years, often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of character development.
  8. The Bildungsroman with a Twist structure — This framework is used to tell a story of a character’s formative years with an unexpected twist or outcome, it is often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of surprise.
  9. The Collage structure — This framework is used to tell a story by piecing together different elements, it can be used to create a sense of fragmentation or to explore different perspectives.
  10. The Confessional structure — This framework is used to tell a story through a character’s confession, it can be used to explore the thoughts and feelings of a character or to create a sense of authenticity.
  11. The Cyclical structure — This framework is used to tell a story that follows a pattern of events that repeat themselves, it can be used to create a sense of inevitability or to explore different themes of repetition and cycle.
  12. The Dreyfus Model of Skill Acquisition — This is not a writing-specific framework, but it can be applied to writing. This model of skill acquisition divides the learning process into five stages: novice, advanced beginner, competent, proficient, and expert. This can be used as a guide for tracking your progress as a writer and identifying areas for improvement.
  13. The Dual-Protagonist structure — This framework is used to tell a story from the point of view of two protagonists, it can be used to create a sense of contrast or to explore different perspectives.
  14. The Dystopian structure — This framework is used to tell a story set in a bleak, oppressive society, it can be used to explore different themes and to create a sense of social commentary.
  15. The Episodic structure — This framework is based on the idea that the story is divided into a series of episodes or vignettes, often used to explore different aspects of the story, it can also be used to create a sense of serialization in the story.
  16. The Epistolary Novel Structure — This framework involves the story being told through letters, diary entries, emails, or other forms of written communication. This structure can give an insight into the inner thoughts of the characters and can be used to build tension and make the story more engaging.
  17. The Epistolary structure — This framework is used to tell a story through a series of letters, diary entries, or other forms of written communication. It can be used to add a sense of authenticity to the story or to explore different perspectives.
  18. The Erotic structure — This framework is used to tell a story with explicit sexual content, often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of sensuality.
  19. The Experimental structure — This framework is used to tell a story in an unconventional or experimental format, it can be used to push the boundaries of storytelling and to create a sense of innovation.
  20. The Five-Point Plot Structure — This framework breaks the story into five parts: the introduction, the rising action, the climax, the falling action, and the resolution. It is often used as a guide for novel writing and allows for a clear progression of the story.
  21. The Five C’s — This framework, created by Michael Hauge, focuses on the five key elements of storytelling: character, conflict, plot, theme, and transformation. It’s intended as a guide for writers to help them create stories that are well-structured and emotionally powerful.
  22. The Five Commandments of Storytelling — This framework, created by Robert McKee, is based on the idea that all successful stories have five elements: character, action, theme, dialogue, and thought. It’s widely used as a guide for screenwriting and novel writing to make sure that the story has all the necessary elements to be a compelling one.
  23. The Five W’s and One H — This framework is a method for organizing and summarizing information that is often used in journalism, but it can also be useful in fiction writing. It consists of answering the questions Who, What, When, Where, Why, and How, this framework can be used to help the writer organize their thoughts, keep track of information and make sure that the story is clear, complete, and consistent.
  24. The Flash Fiction structure — This framework is used to tell a story in a short format, often limited to less than 1000 words, it can be used to explore different themes and to create a sense of immediacy.
  25. The Flashback structure — This framework is used to show events that happened in the past in relation to the current events of the story, it’s used to add depth and meaning to the story, often used to explore the motivations and actions of characters.
  26. The Four-Act Structure — Similar to the Three-Act Structure, this framework breaks a story into four parts: the setup, the rising action, the climax, and the resolution. It is often used as a guide for screenwriting and allows for a more complex and nuanced approach to story structure.
  27. The Four C’s — This framework is similar to the Five C’s, but it focuses on four key elements of storytelling: character, conflict, change, and choice.
  28. The Frame Story structure — This framework involves a story within a story, it’s often used to create a sense of nostalgia, or as a way to explore different perspectives or characters within a single narrative.
  29. The Graphic novel structure — This framework is used to tell a story through a combination of text and illustrations. It’s often used in comics, but it can also be used in other forms of fiction.
  30. The Hero’s Journey — This framework, which was popularized by Joseph Campbell in his book “The Hero with a Thousand Faces”, is a structure for a story that follows a hero as they undertake a journey and faces challenges, leading them to personal growth and self-discovery. It’s widely used in fantasy and epic stories.
  31. The Immersive structure — This framework is used to tell a story in a way that is meant to fully immerse the reader in the story, often using multi-media elements and interactive elements, it is often used to create a sense of realism, and to create a unique experience for the reader
  32. The Improvisational structure — This framework is used to tell a story through improvisation, it can be used to create a sense of spontaneity and to explore different emotions.
  33. The Improvised structure — This framework tells the story in a way that emphasizes the creativity and spontaneity of the writer, often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of unpredictability.
  34. The in media res structure — This framework is a Latin phrase that means “in the midst of things” and it starts a story in the middle of the action, often used to create a sense of immediacy and to grab the reader’s attention from the beginning.
  35. The in medias res with a twist structure — This framework is similar to the in medias res structure, but it includes a twist that changes the interpretation of the events that took place before the story starts. It can be used to create a sense of unpredictability and to explore different perspectives.
  36. The Interactive structure — This framework is used to tell a story in an interactive format, it can be used in video games, choose-your-own-adventure books, and other forms of media.
  37. The Inverted structure — This framework is used to tell a story in reverse chronological order, it can be used to create a sense of intrigue or to explore the consequences of a specific event.
  38. The Künstlerroman structure — This framework is used to tell a story of an artist’s journey and development, often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of character development.
  39. The Linear-Nonlinear structure — This framework combines elements of linear and non-linear storytelling, it can be used to create a sense of intrigue and surprise by jumping back and forth in time.
  40. The Linear Plot Structure — This framework is based on the idea that all stories have a clear beginning, middle, and end, and that the events in the story should be in chronological order. It is often used as a guide for writing short stories and flash fiction.
  41. The Magical Realism structure — This framework is used to tell a story that blends elements of fantasy with a realistic setting, often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of mysticism.
  42. The Mashup structure — This framework is used to tell a story by combining elements from multiple genres or works, it can be used to create a sense of diversity or to explore different themes.
  43. The Mind-Bending structure — This framework is used to tell a story that bends the laws of reality, it can be used to create a sense of intrigue or to explore different perspectives.
  44. The Monologue structure — This framework is used to tell a story through a series of speeches or soliloquies, it can be used to explore the thoughts and feelings of a single character or to create a sense of intimacy with the reader.
  45. The Monomyth — Also known as the hero’s journey, it’s a framework first described by Joseph Campbell in his book ‘The Hero with a Thousand Faces’ that describes the common pattern of adventure and transformation that the protagonist experiences in a narrative work, in which the hero leaves the familiar world to face an unknown one, faces challenges and ultimately reaches a realization about themselves.
  46. The Movie treatment structure — This framework is often used in screenwriting and it’s based on a document that summarizes the story and its elements, it includes a synopsis of the story, the characters, the setting, and a breakdown of the scenes.
  47. The Multi-Media structure — This framework tells a story through multiple forms of media like text, images, videos, and audio which when combined will enhance the story and create a sense of immersion.
  48. The Multi-POV structure — This framework uses multiple points of view in the story, allowing the reader to gain insight into different characters’ thoughts, feelings, and motivations.
  49. The Multiple-Ending structure — This framework is used to provide different possible outcomes for the story, it can be used to create a sense of unpredictability or to explore different possible paths for the characters.
  50. The Multiple-Protagonist structure — This framework is used to tell a story from the point of view of multiple protagonists, it can be used to create a sense of complexity and to explore different perspectives.
  51. The Nested narrative structure — This framework involves a story that is being told inside of another story, it’s used to create a sense of depth and meaning to the story, giving the reader insight into the different perspectives of the characters.
  52. The Non-Fiction structure — This framework is used to tell a true story, it’s often used in memoirs and biographies, but it can also be used in other forms of fiction to create a sense of realism.
  53. The Non-Linear-Chronological structure — This framework is used to tell a story out of chronological order, it can be used to create a sense of intrigue or to explore different aspects of the story.
  54. The Non-Linear-Multiple-Protagonist structure — This framework is used to tell a story through multiple perspectives, in non-linear chronological order, often used to create a sense of complexity and to explore different perspectives.
  55. The Non-Narrative structure — This framework is used to tell a story without a traditional narrative structure, it can be used to create a sense of experimentation or to explore different themes.
  56. The Non-Traditional Narrative structure — This framework is used to tell a story in a format that deviates from traditional narrative structure, often used to create a sense of experimentation and to explore different themes in an unconventional way.
  57. The Novella structure — This framework is used to tell a story of moderate length, often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of intimacy.
  58. The Objective POV structure — this framework uses a narrator that doesn’t show the thoughts or feelings of any characters and is limited to the observable actions and dialogue of the characters.
  59. The Omniscient POV structure — This framework uses the POV of an all-knowing narrator, it’s often used in literary fiction, epic stories, and fantasy. It gives the author control of the story, showing the thoughts and feelings of multiple characters and giving an overall view of the story.
  60. The One-Sentence structure — This framework is used to tell a story in a single sentence, it can be used to create a sense of brevity or to explore deeper meaning in a concise format.
  61. The Open-Ended structure — This framework is used to leave the ending of the story open for interpretation, allowing the reader to form their own conclusions about the events of the story.
  62. The Oral storytelling structure — This framework is based on the tradition of oral storytelling, it’s often used in folktales, myths, and legends, and it’s based on the idea of a storyteller passing on a story to an audience through spoken word.
  63. The Pantomime structure — This framework is used to tell a story through physical movements and gestures, it’s often used in dance and theater, but it can also be used in other forms of fiction.
  64. The Parable structure — This framework is used to tell a story that teaches a moral or lesson. It’s often used in religious or allegorical stories.
  65. The Picaresque structure — This framework is used to tell a story of a roguish protagonist’s journey through different adventures, often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of realism.
  66. The Play Structure — This framework is based on the structure of a stage play, it’s often used in theater and can be adapted to other forms of storytelling as well, it’s based on the idea of the story being divided into acts and scenes.
  67. The Plot Mountain — This framework is a visual representation of a story, where the plot builds up to the climax, the highest point of the mountain, and then descends to the resolution. It is often used as a guide for story structure and pacing.
  68. The Podcast structure — This framework is used to tell a story through audio recordings, it’s often used in audio dramas and non-fiction storytelling, but it can also be used in other forms of fiction.
  69. The Poetic structure — This framework is used to tell a story through poetry, it can be used to create a sense of beauty or to explore deeper meaning in the story.
  70. The Puzzle Structure — This framework tells a story in a way that will force the reader to use problem-solving skills in order to figure out the story. It is often used to create a sense of mystery, challenge the reader, and keep them engaged.
  71. The Question and Answer structure — This framework is used to tell a story by asking and answering a series of questions, it can be used to create a sense of intrigue or to explore the thoughts and feelings of a character.
  72. The Recursive structure — This framework is used to explore the same event from different perspectives, often used to create a sense of intrigue and surprise, it can also be used to create a sense of cyclicality in the story.
  73. The Role-Playing Game structure — This framework is used to tell a story through a game format, it can be used to create a sense of interactivity and to explore different perspectives.
  74. The Rondeau structure — This framework is used to tell a story through a specific verse form, it can be used to create a sense of musicality and to explore different emotions.
  75. The Sandbox structure — This framework is used to tell a story through a non-linear, open-world format, it can be used to create a sense of freedom and to explore different possibilities.
  76. The Save the Cat Beats — This framework is an extension of the Save the Cat Structure and it is a guide for structuring the plot of a screenplay. Snyder describes 15 beats that the story must hit, it will ensure that the story has a clear progression and allows the writer to maintain focus on the story’s structure.
  77. The Scenario structure — This framework is used to tell a story through a series of scenarios, it can be used to explore different possibilities and to create a sense of unpredictability.
  78. The Screenplay structure — This framework is used to tell a story for the purpose of being adapted for film or television, it can be used to create a sense of visual storytelling.
  79. The Second-Person structure — This framework is used to tell a story in the second-person point of view, it’s often used to create a sense of immediacy and to put the reader in the story.
  80. The Sestina structure — This framework is used to tell a story through a specific verse form, it can be used to create a sense of repetition and to explore different emotions.
  81. The Seven-Point Story Structure — This framework is based on seven key stages of a story: the inciting incident, the progressive complications, the crisis, the climax, the falling action, the resolution, and the conclusion. This structure is widely used in screenplays and it’s a great tool for plot development
  82. The Seven-Point Story Structure — this is a structure that breaks down a story into seven key elements: the setup, the inciting incident, the first plot point, the midpoint, the second plot point, the climax, and the resolution.
  83. The Seven-Sequence Structure — This framework is based on dividing the story into seven sequences, which are defined by the main event and the main character’s reaction to it. It’s often used as a guide to help writers understand the progression of the story and make sure that the story has a logical and clear flow.
  84. The Snowball Method — This method is similar to the Snowflake Method, but it emphasizes starting with a small idea and adding more details and complexity as the story progresses, instead of starting with a detailed and complex idea and simplifying it.
  85. The Snowflake Method — This method, created by novelist Randy Ingermanson, is a step-by-step process for creating a detailed and well-structured story, starting with a basic idea and expanding it into a full-fledged novel. It encourages the writer to take a detailed, incremental approach to develop their story, creating a “snowflake” that begins as a single, simple idea and becomes more complex and detailed as the writer works through the steps.
  86. The Song-lyric structure — This framework is used to tell a story through the lyrics of a song, it can be used to create a sense of immediacy and to explore different emotions.
  87. The Soundscape structure — This framework is used to tell a story through a combination of sounds, it can be used to create a sense of atmosphere and to explore different emotions.
  88. The Split-Narrative structure — This framework is used to tell a story from multiple perspectives, it can be used to create a sense of depth and to explore the thoughts and feelings of different characters.
  89. The Split-Timeline structure — This framework uses two or more timelines that run parallel to each other, it allows the author to explore different characters and events, adding layers of complexity to the story.
  90. The Story Circle — This method, created by novelist and screenwriter Michael Hauge, is based on the idea that all stories can be broken down into a simple, universal structure, consisting of a character who wants something, and must overcome obstacles in order to get it. The story circle is a circle, divided into eight stages, that goes from the character’s ordinary world to the inciting incident, the progressive complications, and the crisis, leading to the climax and the resolution of the story.
  91. The Story Grid — This framework, created by Shawn Coyne, is based on the idea that all successful stories have a specific structure and that the structure can be broken down into distinct elements. The Story Grid is a detailed, step-by-step process for analyzing and improving the structure of a story, it focuses on the premise, the genre, and the global story of the story.
  92. The Story Spine — This is a simple and flexible framework that can be used to quickly map out the basic structure of a story. It consists of a single sentence that summarizes the story and is often used as a starting point for more detailed story development.
  93. The Stream-of-Unconsciousness structure — This framework is similar to the Stream-of-consciousness structure, but it emphasizes the representation of the characters’ subconscious thoughts and feelings.
  94. The Stream-of-consciousness structure — This framework is based on the author’s thoughts and impressions being represented in a free-flowing, unpunctuated manner, often used to represent the inner thoughts and feelings of characters and to give the story a sense of realism.
  95. The Subjective POV structure — This framework uses the POV of a single character, it allows the author to immerse the reader in the character’s thoughts and feelings but can be limiting in terms of the scope of the story.
  96. The Surreal structure — This framework is used to tell a story that blends elements of reality and dream-like imagery, often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of mysticism.
  97. The Thematic structure — This framework is based on the story being structured around a specific theme, it’s often used to explore different aspects of a subject and to give the story a deeper meaning.
  98. The Three-Act Paradigm — This framework is similar to the Three-Act Structure, but it focuses on the underlying theme or message of the story. It’s based on the idea that every story can be boiled down to a single, central question that the story answers. The three acts represent the setup, the confrontation, and the resolution of the central question, it’s often used to understand the deeper meaning of the story
  99. The Three-Act Story Structure with Four Plot Points — This framework builds upon the traditional three-act structure, but it also includes four key plot points: the inciting incident, the first turning point, the second turning point, and the climax. It allows for a more detailed approach to story structure and character development
  100. The Three-Act Structure — This classic structure, which is widely used in both film and literature, is divided into three parts: the setup (Act 1), the confrontation (Act 2), and the resolution (Act 3). The three-act structure is a flexible framework that can be applied to a wide variety of stories and is often used as a starting point for more detailed story development.
  101. The Three-Act Structure with a Midpoint — This framework is similar to the traditional three-act structure but it includes a midpoint that divides the second act into two parts, allowing for a more detailed approach to character development and plot progression.
  102. The Three-Act Structure with a Twist — This framework adds an additional twist to the traditional three-act structure by introducing a subplot that runs parallel to the main plot and adds an additional layer of complexity to the story.
  103. The Three-Phase Plot Structure — This framework breaks the story into three phases: The Setup, The Confrontation, and The Resolution. It is often used as a guide for novel writing and allows for a clear progression of the story.
  104. The Three-Step Structure — This is a simple framework that focuses on three main elements, the setup, the confrontation, and the resolution. It’s widely used as a guide to help writers keep the focus on the plot of the story and make sure that the story has a clear beginning, middle, and end.
  105. The Time Loop structure — This framework involves a story in which the same events occur over and over again in a cyclical manner, often used as a tool for building suspense and exploring the theme of fate and destiny.
  106. The Tragedy structure — This framework is used to tell a story that ends in disaster, often used to explore different themes and to create a sense of catharsis.
  107. The Trivia-Mystery structure — This framework is often used in mystery stories and it’s based on revealing piece by piece the elements of the mystery through a set of clues, the reader has to piece together the information in order to solve the mystery.
  108. The Unreliable Narrator structure — this framework uses a narrator that’s not trustworthy, the reader can’t be sure of the veracity of what’s being related, it’s often used as a tool for building suspense and adding a layer of complexity to the story.
  109. The Utopian structure — This framework is used to tell a story set in a perfect, ideal society, it can be used to explore different themes and to create a sense of social commentary.
  110. The Verse-Chorus structure — This framework is used to tell a story through a specific verse-chorus format, it can be used to create a sense of musicality and to explore different emotions.
  111. The Video game structure — This framework is used to tell a story through an interactive format, often used to create a sense of player agency and to explore different choices and outcomes.
  112. The Writer’s Journey — This framework is based on the Hero’s journey, but in this case, the protagonist is not a hero but the writer herself, and the hero’s journey is the journey of the writer to create the story. This structure is often associated with the career of writers and the creative process.
