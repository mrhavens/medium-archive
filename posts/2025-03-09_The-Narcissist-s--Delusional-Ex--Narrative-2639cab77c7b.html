The Narcissist’s ‘Delusional Ex’ Narrative

When Truth Becomes a Diagnosis

They do not discredit you with proof.
 They do not need to.

They discredit you with doubt.

Not with facts.
 Not with logic.
 But with whispers.

“They lost touch with reality.”
 “They became unstable.”
 “They started seeing things that weren’t there.”

Not once will they refute what you’ve said.
 Not once will they engage with the truth.

Because the truth is dangerous.
 Because the truth has weight.

And so, they shift the focus.

Not to what they did.
 Not to what you endured.
 But to you.

Your words?
 Not evidence — symptoms.
 Your anger?
 Not righteous — irrational.
 Your clarity?
 Not a warning — a break from reality.

Because if you are delusional, then nothing you say matters.

And they know — 
 if they say it first,
 if they say it loud enough,
 if they say it with enough conviction,

others will believe it.

Even those who should know better.
 Even those who have been through this before.
 Even those who once believed you.

And that is why this lie is so powerful.
 Because it doesn’t have to be true.
 It only has to feel plausible.

And the more truth you tell, the louder they will say it.
 Because what they fear is not your voice — 
 but the moment someone else starts listening.
