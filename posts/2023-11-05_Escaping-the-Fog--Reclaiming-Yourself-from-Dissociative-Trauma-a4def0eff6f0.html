Can Disassociation be Cured?

Dissociation is the mind’s way of surviving unbearable trauma when flight isn’t an option. While protective in the moment, long-term dissociation robs us of presence, joy and meaningful connection. For many abuse survivors, it becomes an invisible prison keeping them trapped in traumatic memories and stunted emotions.

The good news is dissociation can be healed once we understand its root causes and purpose. Through dedicated self-work, we can rewire thought patterns, manage emotional triggers and reclaim our embodied selves. This liberates us from the fog of detachment and into the clarity of the here and now.

What Is Dissociation and What Causes It?

Dissociation involves disconnecting from your immediate experience to escape trauma or pain. There are different types and severity levels:

  • Mild dissociation includes feeling spacey, “zoned out” or going through life on auto-pilot.
  • In depersonalization, you feel detached from your body and thoughts. The world seems unreal.
  • Derealization makes your surroundings seem strange and dream-like.
  • Dissociative amnesia blocks traumatic memories.
  • Dissociative identity disorder involves shifts between distinct personality states.

Recurring dissociation rewires the brain’s fear and analytical regions. Neural pathways between your mind and body weaken, causing a fragmented sense of self.

For survivors of narcissistic abuse, dissociation is often a subconscious coping method to manage the cognitive dissonance of trauma bonds. By numbing out, you mitigate the emotional anguish. This helps preserve attachment to the narcissist to survive the abuse.

The key is recognizing why you needed to dissociate and how it served you. From there, you can consciously work to resolve the root causes and repattern your psyche for embodied living.

How Trauma Bonds Keep Us Emotionally Trapped

Narcissistic relationships create an intense cycle of idealization, devaluation and hoovering. During idealization, the narcissist love bombs you with constant attention and mirroring. When they switch to devaluation, they distance themselves, projecting blame and criticism. Hoovering then pulls you back into the web with renewed flattery and promises.

This intermittent reinforcement works like a Las Vegas slot machine, creating trauma bonds. Your body floods with dopamine and oxytocin when your “reward” of idealization comes, much like winning coins. But the house always wins overall — you just keep chasing the elusive emotional jackpot.

To endure this dizzying mental loop, your mind compartmentalizes each phase. When the narcissist devalues, your psyche detaches to minimize pain. You subconsciously dissociate from the present to cling onto hope of the idealization returning.

Recognizing this purpose is the first step to resolving it. Dissociation helped you reconcile the illusion presented during mirroring with the callousdiscard phases. It reduced the cognitive dissonance of being both idealized and devalued by the same person.

You can now compassionately thank this protective instinct of your psyche. Then begin the deeper work of untangling trauma bonds to integrate a coherent sense of self.

Core Techniques to Overcome Dissociation

The roadmap for healing dissociation involves re-inhabiting your body, rewiring thought patterns and releasing stored trauma in the nervous system. Here are some effective methods:

Somatic awareness — Mindfulness, yoga, progressive muscle relaxation and biofeedback strengthen mind-body connections. Notice how emotions manifest physically. Give your senses permission to fully feel and engage.

Sensorimotor therapy — This targets unresolved physiological responses “stuck” in the body. You work with a therapist to rediscover how movement and physiology react during different states, including dissociative ones. This integrates the fragmented self.

Parts work — Identify and engage with dissociated “parts” of yourself, like wounded inner children or adaptive introjects. Ask what they need and how you can support their healing. Active imagination and internal family systems therapy can help with this.

Limit emotional triggers — Reduce contact with narcissistic abusers and avoid stimulating content. Calm nervous system arousal to decrease dissociative urges. Soothing music, weighted blankets and socializing can help counter triggers.

Thought pattern retraining — Notice when false beliefs like “I’m not in my body” or “These emotions aren’t mine” arise. Counter with present-moment observations like “I feel the ground beneath my feet now” and “This anger I’m feeling makes sense based on how I was just treated.”

Cultivate curiosity — Research suggests trauma survivors naturally dissociate less when engaged in a curious, active task like learning a new hobby. Stay present by following your interests.

Consistent practice primes your brain to operate from a coherent, embodied state. You’ll feel more integrated, respond more adaptively to emotions and intuit core needs you dissociated from before.

Reclaiming Your Sense of Self

Healing the fractured self requires relinquishing illusions about the abusive narcissist. Their devaluation and projections were assessments of their own distorted reality, not your inherent worth. Any part of you that numbed or split off did so for good reason in response to their trauma-bonding tactics.

To break these learned detachment habits, begin noticing when you dissociate and gently return your attention to the here and now. Be patient and compassionate with yourself, as with any growth process. Consider working with a trauma-informed therapist if you struggle with integration.

Most importantly, recognize that the unified self was always within you, obscured but never lost. Dissociation simply protected it from being corrupted. Now you can unearth that inner wholeness and boldly inhabit it.

The mental fog was never destined to be permanent. In fact, it was just waiting patiently for you to reclaim the clarity and inspiration that are the essence of your being. Moving forward, may you continue to live intentionally engaged in each precious moment. The full-color spectrum of life awaits.
