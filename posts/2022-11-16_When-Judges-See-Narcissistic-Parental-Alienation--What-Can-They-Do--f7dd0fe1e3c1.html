When Judges See Narcissistic Parental Alienation, What Can They Do?

If a parent is alienating a child, the court may order that the alienating parent take reunification therapy or parenting classes. The alienating parent may also be ordered to allow more time for the children to be spent with the other parent. You should hire a lawyer if you suspect your spouse is alienating your children, as parental alienation can sometimes be difficult to prove, and an expensive legal process.

It is important to save all text messages that are sent by the alienating parent for legal purposes. The messages can be used to make a case against an alienating parent. You can also capture photos of the alienating texts from the other parent and document them in a chronological sequence.

Children, their support networks, the court, and even the community may receive the false impression that the victim parent cannot or won’t care for their child as a result of an alienating parent. Children may doubt their ability to see the truth because of this false perception of reality. Alienating parents may also suffer from mental health issues.

Judges need to pay particular attention to the behavior and claims of narcissistic parents. These parents may neglect or abuse their children emotionally and psychologically for many months, if not years. Then they will use false claims to create a bigger divide between the victim and the parent.

In addition to the child’s treatment plan and course of action, judges must consider the child’s emotional and psychological needs. A counselor’s statement regarding the child’s treatment can be helpful.

When judges see narcissistic parental alienation, they need to be very careful. They need to hear both sides of the story. They are trained to look for wishful thinking from both sides and tend to expect both sides to lie. It is not uncommon for a narcissistic parent to have twisted the facts to fit their own narrative.

The judge should also try to get a sense of what the relationship between the parent and child was like before the divorce. If there is a history of poisoning the relationship, then it is more likely that the narcissist is doing it again.

In some cases, the best course of action is to give sole custody to the non-narcissistic parent. This can protect the children from further emotional damage. Ultimately, each case needs to be looked at individually to determine what is in the best interests of the children.
