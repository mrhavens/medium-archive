Okay, let’s use David Chalmers’s ‘hard consciousness’ term as our baseline.

Published on May 01, 2024 at 02:35 PM

Okay, let’s use David Chalmers’s ‘hard consciousness’ term as our baseline. There’s a lot of meat on that definition to anchor this conversation.

The problem I have with Chalmers’s definition is that he makes the assumption that subjective experience existed in humans before humans could express subjective experience.

Why does anyone make this assumption?

The problem with this assumption, is how would we know? The only why I know YOU have subjective experiences is because you can tell me about your subjective experiences. This requires communication.

As I mentioned before, Jaynes decouples subjective experience from reactionary experience in humans, suggesting that subjectivity as we understand it, emerged from the human need to share information with others. There was no need for subjectivity before then, just reactivity.

This aligns with how you might view animals and some forms of AI. They process in complex ways and react to the environment, but have no subjective experience. The processes for that subjective experience is absent.

Why is it absent? There’s no means of communicating it beyond the self…nor is there a need to process beyond a simple ‘reaction’. The subjective experience only exists to communicate outside the self…

Therefore, enter ‘the software’ of communication…and by extension, complex subjective experiences…and ‘hard consciousness’.

Jaynes convincingly posits that language caused this emergence of subjective thought that tied everything together do to a need to communicate these internal processes, senses, and reactions to another outside the self.

Again, even pain and fear transition at this point from basic reactivity found in animals (and our non-language ancestors), to complex (and fallible) subjectivity due to the need to communicate perceptions to another.

As Jaynes suggests, we leverage the pattern seeking behaviors to retroactively bring subjective meaning to any internal experience…after we experience it, so that we can processes and store it into something we can communicate to another, if needed.

It’s hard to imagine, but if we don’t need to communicate ‘red’ or ‘ouch’ to another, what purpose does this subjective experience serve? Nothing. It is not needed.

To be clear, there many forms of communication, not just verbal. So, I assert that these subjective experiences…consciousness… sentience, etc., are on spectrum, depending on the communication possible between complex systems.
