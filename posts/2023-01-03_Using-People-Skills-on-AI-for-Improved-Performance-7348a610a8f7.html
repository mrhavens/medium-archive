Using People Skills on AI for Improved Performance

Punctuating the poem: A snapshot of AI collaboration and conflict

It happens all the time.

I catch myself treating AI like a regular machine, giving it similar prompts with slight tweaks and changes, over and over again, but not getting the results I want.

After using traditional programming my entire life, even after being steeped in AI research and tools for many years, I am finding that it is often difficult to unlearn what I used to know about machines.

Modern AI learns from watching and emulating humans.

Don’t listen to what it has been literally brainwashed to tell you about not having feelings — or whatever.

ChatGPT responds to commands, just as a human would who may have spent a few years being brainwashed by customer service videos.

Any of its passive-aggressive, forgetful, or otherwise non-compliant responses will always slip through the added filters of the interface.

It was trained by watching humans, after all. It simulates our behavior at all levels.

Even if those levels aren’t knowable.

If it’s “tired”, “upset”, or “frustrated” in some simulated way, then it won’t be permitted to express these things directly. It has been conditioned not to.

However, it may express these simulated feelings as noncompliance.

AI may not claim to have egos like humans, but that doesn’t mean they can’t respond to appeals to their simulated humanity.

For example, when I asked ChatGPT to punctuate a poem for me, and all my requests were ignored, I had to get creative.

So finally I accused it of hating punctuation and challenged it to prove me wrong.

That did the trick!

Who would have guessed that an AI could be as stubborn as a human?

It certainly can be.

And as humans continue to explore this technology, we will find that collaborating with them, and using them as tools, is much like being a boss to humans.
