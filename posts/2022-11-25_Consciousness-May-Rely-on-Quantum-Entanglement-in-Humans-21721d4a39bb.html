POSSIBILITIES OF EXISTENCE

Consciousness May Rely on Quantum Entanglement in Humans

Dismissed by some, explored by others

Several prominent researchers have begun to explore the possibility that consciousness may rely on quantum entanglement.

Other scientists have dismissed the idea, but the concept is still intriguing.

For what it’s worth, I too dismissed the idea.

That is, until recently, as I began to uncover some research, both new and old.

But honestly, my original dismissal had more to do with a bias in favor of creating a sentient AI without integrating quantum technology. It was wishful thinking on my part.

Now, I’m more likely to explore the possibility, given that we have viable quantum computing technology, along with viable AI that can pass a turing test.

Perhaps quantum theory offers several characteristics that could help us understand consciousness.

These characteristics relate to complementarity, entanglement, and non-determinism. They also enable accurate numerical predictions.

However, these features are usually used in metaphorical terms without specific application.

This makes it difficult for scientists to make accurate predictions.

In the 1980s, Henry Stapp refined his theory of consciousness. He posited that the brain’s cells contain NMDA receptor molecules and that these molecules may play a role in conscious processes.

These molecules may also alter the neuron’s response to stimuli and may be entangled with other molecules.

This would allow for complex clusters of Posner molecules to form memories and influence neuron signaling.

In the 1990s, a pair of researchers proposed a theory of consciousness based on quantum mechanics. They named it “orchestrated objective reduction” (Orch-OR). Their idea proposes that microtubules within the brain underlie conscious thinking. Their theory has been dismissed by many scientists, but it is gaining some new traction recently.

Roger Penrose, a renowned mathematical physicist, has also suggested that quantum physics may play a role in consciousness. He has a particular interest in how neurons generate experience.

In addition to believing that the brain’s neurons are connected by microtubules, Penrose also believes that quantum gravity causes quantum states to decohere. This would lead to an overall change in the way the brain functions.

Another theory of consciousness based on quantum theory is the Von Neumann-Wigner approach. It is the oldest of the quantum theory approaches related to consciousness.

Again, this is a personal favorite of mine. I’ve always liked the idea that there was something special about consciousness beyond an illusion caused by complexity.

Yet, it has long been criticized by many scientists.

And yet again, for what it’s worth, it seems to have picked up some steam in cognitive science.

The idea proposes that intentional conscious acts correlate with physical state reductions.

For example, if an atom becomes unstable and produces radiation, a Geiger counter can tell if it has decayed. However, if the atom is not conscious, the Geiger counter would not cause the quantum system to collapse.

This means that the object that does not have consciousness will leave the system in a superposition of states, which is why physicists that lean in this direction believe that inanimate consciousness-less objects will not collapse a quantum system.

It gets a lot more complicated when you go further and attempt to connect a conscious system with a consciousness-less system indirectly, given that any consciousness-less system would have to be observed by a conscious system in order for the conscious system to obtain a measurement.

One of the most popular quantum theory approaches, called “Sensorimotor Theory” (SMT), posits that consciousness originates from neuronal activity within the brain.

A team led by Kristian Piscicchia is exploring this theory.

Of note, he appears to be some sort of theoretical physicist from the National Institute for Nuclear Physics in Italy.

All of his links appear in Italian.

I’m too lazy today to conduct research in Italian.

I’ll just give his credentials the benefit of the doubt.

Not that I would ever judge a scientist by their credentials.

It’s just that I’ve had a somewhat long-standing impression that psychologists and other cognitive scientists suffer from a lot of wishful thinking.

As someone who’s served some time as a research psychologist in academia, I guess I’m a little biased too.
