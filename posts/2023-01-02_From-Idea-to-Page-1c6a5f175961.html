From Idea to Page

Simple logline templates for turning your story into reality

If you’re a writer looking to craft compelling stories, you might be familiar with the “Save the Cat” method of screenwriting.

Of course, it’s not just useful for screenwriting.

It’s useful for novels, too.

…for pretty much any story, really.

In his book “Save the Cat! The Last Book on Screenwriting You’ll Ever Need,” Blake Snyder outlines a number of techniques and tools that writers can use to create engaging and satisfying narratives.

One of these tools is the logline — a one-sentence summary of a story that includes the main character, their goal, and the conflict they face.

It’s a combination of the words “log,” which refers to a record of a ship’s speed, course, and distance traveled, and “line,” which refers to a statement or series of statements that capture the essence of something.

In the publishing industry, loglines are often used as a tool to pitch novels to agents and editors and serve as a way to quickly and concisely convey the key elements of a story.

Loglines can be particularly useful for novelists who are looking to condense their story into a single, compelling sentence that will grab the attention of industry professionals and potential readers.

The word “logline” has been around for a while and is widely used by writers as a way to summarize and pitch their stories.

To help you craft compelling loglines for your own stories, I’ve compiled a collection of logline templates that are inspired by Snyder’s “Save the Cat” method.

These templates will help you to highlight the key elements of your story and craft a logline that captures the essence of your narrative.
