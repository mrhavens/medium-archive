Learning From My Writing Mistakes: A Postmortem of My Worst Performing Articles of 2022

Exploring the blind spots and valuable learning opportunities in my writing journey

Kicking myself in the ass in public has to be healthy on some level, but I’m no therapist.

I’m not particularly excited to share these reflections and insights with the world, as a relatively new writer on Medium, but I’m going to do it anyway.

Making mistakes is an inevitable part of the learning process.

It’s through our mistakes that we truly grow and improve.

Really.

I believe that…

Yup.

I. Believe. That.

Ahem.

I suppose that’s why I’ve decided to dive into this postmortem of my worst-performing articles — to gain a deeper understanding of what didn’t work and how I can do better in the future.

Maybe you will see something I missed.

Maybe you can help me learn the error of my ways.

Feel free.

But more likely, maybe you will gain some insight.

Maybe you can avoid in your own writing, whatever the hell it was that I did completely wrong.

Each piece of crap writing I put out into the world — whether it be rejected, unviewed, or unread — presents an opportunity for self-reflection and self-improvement.

This is especially true whenever I think whatever I’ve got mostly finished is good enough to publish.

While I never set out to produce shit work, it’s important to acknowledge and learn from any missteps along the way.

Maybe it’s not really shit. Maybe it just hasn’t found an audience.

I won’t know unless I make a ton more mistakes. And keep making them.

I’ve got my blind spots.

Big ones.

We all do.

That’s why I often turn to metrics, artificial intelligence, and you guys to help me identify areas for growth and development as a writer.

So, without further ado, I invite you to join me in exploring the most valuable learning opportunities I’ve encountered on this platform by diving into my absolute shittiest, lowest-performing work of 2022.

Title: Are You a Writer Who Is Afraid of AI?

One sentence synopsis: AI may be able to automate some writing tasks, but it still can’t quite match the wit and creativity of a human writer (thank goodness!)

For this article, I decided to use an AI-generated image of a robot on a typewriter.

I think it’s awesome.

Unfortunately, it never gained an audience.

I was trying to target a specific group of people who might be a little hesitant about artificial intelligence, but let’s be real — who wants to read an article about something they’re afraid of?

I’m guessing the distribution algorithm for the platform I published on probably filtered it out, too.

And trying to convince people to embrace their fears?

Not exactly a winning marketing strategy, especially if you’re not in the self-help industry.

It’s totally possible the only view I got was from the editor of the publication I submitted it to, who ultimately rejected it.

But hey, at least I tried!

Me: Describe in a single sentence how this article can be improved.
ChatGPT: To improve the writing in the article, the organization and structure could be refined and the language and style improved, and it could include more concrete examples and practical tips.

Well, damn.

ChatGPT just said my writing is shit.

You all saw it.

I’ve got blind spots, as I’ve already stated.

Maybe it does too.

Not many though. I can attest.

But it didn’t once talk about what I think is wrong with the article.

Ahem.

But, that’s sort of the point of AI collaboration.

The damn thing is a lot smarter than me.

I’ve gotta respect that.

Title: A Stagecoach to Ximes

One sentence synopsis: Darago is frequently agitated and tense while on a bumpy stagecoach ride with Sam, a young, repetitively storytelling stagecoach driver who always seems oblivious to Darago’s inflections and discomfort.

I’ve gotta admit it…

I put out into the interwebs, an incomplete piece of writing.

This was more of a character development exercise than a story.

In hindsight, a better approach might have been to write about the process of developing the characters and create a toolkit based on my approach.

But let’s be real, a story with a subtitle like “A somewhat short, frequently uninteresting, often agitating, always tense, and anticipatingly bumpy nonadventure” is pretty much a deterrent for both readers and algorithms alike.

The irony of it all? One person spent more time clapping for this story than all of the total combined time of everyone else reading it.

Just a friendly reminder that some people clap for stories without actually reading them.

This type of mismatched engagement is not overlooked by us writers.

Nope.

Not at all.

But thanks for the engagement nonetheless!

Me: Provide a simple one-sentence analysis about what can be improved in this story to better engage readers.
ChatGPT: To better engage readers, the story could incorporate more plot development and character development to give the reader a reason to care about the characters and events in the story.

Well, damn.

I guess I knew as much.

And the AI agrees.

Title: Portraying the Covert Narcissist in Fiction: Understanding Their Psychology and Tactics

One sentence synopsis: A fiction writer aims to accurately portray a covert narcissist character by understanding their psychology and motivations and creating a relatable and sympathetic character to educate readers and raise awareness of their manipulative tactics.

I submitted this article to a Medium publication and was hoping it would hold people’s attention for longer than it did.

I mean, it was at least accepted for publication by an editor… who didn’t read all of it.

Who could blame them? We’re all too busy to read everything.

It’s possible the topic was a little too specific and didn’t attract a wide audience.

I think this article was one of my attempts at turning my brainstorming and character development work into something useful, but I guess it wasn’t quite engaging enough to hold people’s attention.

While writing about a narcissistic character in fiction might be one of my passion pursuits, it’s probably not for everyone. It’s possible that I could generalize the insight, or maybe frame it in a way that’s more accessible.

Personally, I find leveraging psychobabble a good shortcut when talking to AIs about character development. It understands everything I throw at it, provided I know what I’m talking about. But it might not resonate with average writers.

Hey-ya, ChatGPT?

Me: How does the use of references to terms in psychology make this article more or less clear?
ChatGPT: Using psychological terms and concepts in the article helps the reader gain a more detailed and precise understanding of covert narcissism in fiction, but it is important for the writer to define and explain these terms for readers who may not be familiar with them.

Well, damn.

I guess I never explained anything.

To a general audience, this article was… word salad.

Honestly, that only slightly crossed my mind.
