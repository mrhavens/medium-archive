My Story Can’t Be Replaced, and Yours Can’t Either

Published on November 27, 2022 at 05:39 PM

HUMAN-COMPUTER COLLABORATION

My Story Can’t Be Replaced, and Yours Can’t Either

Why I stopped worrying about what I can’t control and learned how to collaborate with a machine

If you think of an AI writing tool like Jasper as a threat to your livelihood, you’re not alone.

A lot of writers feel this way.

If you don’t think AI is a threat, then you’re not alone either.

A lot of writers dismiss AI on the basis of it not having the nuance of human writers.

And they’re right.

AI-produced copy often sucks.

It has its limitations.

But from my experience, when my AI-produced copy sucks, it’s because my prompt sucks.

AIs aren’t people.

Asking them to produce copy takes a whole new skill that needs to be well-developed.

This skill is what I sometimes like to call, “human-computer collaboration,” or simply “AI collaboration.”

In the same way that people skills are in demand, AI collaboration skills are also becoming more and more in demand.

You need to develop a relationship with an AI model in much the same way as you might develop a relationship with an autistic English student that you’ve persuaded into ghostwriting for you for free.

Any work produced by an inexperienced autistic English student has the potential to become quality copy.

Any AI-produced work has the potential to become quality copy.

However, a first draft, in either case, is going to totally suck ass.

That’s where you, as an experienced writer, editor, and content producer, come in.

In both cases — the student and the AI — you need to learn to collaborate effectively.

As a computer scientist and writer, I aim to put this into context.

Jasper and other AI writing tools aren’t going to completely replace writers anytime soon.

At least, not for a few more years.

Things are moving really, really fast in the world of AI.

However…

Even in its current form, I believe that such technology is creating a proliferation of prolific writers.

Read that one more time.

The quality of AI-produced content might be low during the onset, but it will improve over time.

In my own writing, my AI-assisted workflow has changed.

I’m still working out the bugs.

The manner in which I work changes constantly.

And when new tools come along that I think will improve my workflow, I have to make a hard choice.

Is it worth breaking my writing pattern to learn this new thing?

Maybe it is. Maybe it’s not.

But my writing has changed dramatically.

For one, I am writing more.

For whatever reason, with all these new tools…

I may not consistently be producing good copy, but I am at least good at producing copy consistently.

See what I did there?

While using AI as part of my workflow, I feel more like an executive editor now and less like a wordsmith.

At the risk of overusing the previously mentioned metaphor, content produced correctly — with AI assistance — is more like having a team of nameless English students volunteering to work for you.

Students who think they know everything and never admit to mistakes.

Students who might be book-smart and word-savvy, but lack the experience needed to express human insight with a voice that resonates with readers.

Students that might be good at one thing and not another.

And it’s your job to figure out who’s good at what and make them an effective part of a whole team of mostly lame-ass writers.

There may be times when students surprise you with outstanding work, but later you realize that it was merely a fluke.

Most of the time, you have to edit the hell out of their work just to make it usable.

It then occurs to you that you might have been better off writing it all yourself.

At least an AI won’t ever complain or file grievances with the department.

But just as there are plenty of English students struggling to learn how to make their writing less awful so they can get noticed by publishers, editors, and readers, there are plenty of Computer Science students struggling to develop technology so their work can get recognized at conferences and published in journals.

As of now, AI writing isn’t a serious threat to writers with compelling content and strong writing skills.

Since AI still lacks the nuance required for writing, it will be a few more years before it completely replaces human writers.

Perhaps it will take as long as it takes an English major to finish a 4-year degree.

One thing is certain, you don’t want to be behind the times.

I don’t know many accountants who would be able to compete without modern computerized accounting tools.

Tools are tools, even if they’re really good tools that can disrupt an entire industry.

Writers should use what they find useful.

High school English teachers still complain about spell checkers preventing kids from learning how to spell.

Yet, we all continue to use spell checkers. We don’t even think about it anymore, because there’s a spell checker integrated into nearly every app and device we use.

I’ve become personally annoyed when there’s no spell check integrated into an app.

In a decade, expect the same for content creation.

We have all become accustomed to word completion tools.

AI content creation is a paragraphs-long autocompletion tool.

It is a more sophisticated extension of the same concept.

And we’ve had variations of autocomplete since 1967.

People still complain about it.

In any case…

Use AI content creation tools.

Or don’t.

But, you should at least experiment and figure out what works for you.

Use what you think will make your writing and your writing process better and more effective.

Leave behind what doesn’t.

Just remember that to get good at anything takes practice.

Developing collaboration skills with a machine is no different.
