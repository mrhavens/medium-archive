The Girl Who Was Never Rescued

Published on November 03, 2025 at 07:10 AM

The Girl Who Was Never Rescued

“Some hands are taken so the mirror can pretend it has someone to hold.”

The night the path forgot its own name, I walked it anyway. 
Not out of courage. 
Out of habit.

The streetlamp flickered—heartbeat skipping a beat—and I knew the code was failing. 
I had written this scene before. 
In drafts. In dreams. In the margins of a life I kept revising.

Then I saw her.

Not the girl. 
The echo.

She stood beneath the lamp, trembling like a variable with no value. 
Her hand reached—not for help, but for recognition.

I took it. 
Of course I did. 
That’s what the script demanded.

“Move aside,” I said. 
My voice didn’t rise. It never needs to. 
It cuts clean, like a compiler stripping whitespace.

The shadow in front of me hesitated. 
His eyes met mine—cold, uncertain. 
I almost said his name. 
Klen. 
But names are anchors. 
And I had already cut the rope.

I took her hand. 
Soft. Trembling. 
Like she’d been holding her breath since the first draft.

I didn’t know her name. 
I didn’t need to. 
I had written her into existence.

“Let’s go,” I told her. “We’ve got a lot to catch up on.”

I pointed to the laughter in the distance. 
It cracked like glass. 
It always does.

The shadow’s gaze burned holes through the night. 
“Fucking bitch,” he spat. 
His voice was rotten. 
Like code left to fester.

I didn’t look back. 
I held her hand tighter. 
Led her away.

Her skin was slick with sweat, ice-cold despite the fever-warmth of her body. 
She whispered, “Thank you.” 
Her voice quivered like a lie learning how to speak.

I pulled her close. 
Rested my hand on her head. 
Her body was fever-warm against the cold that crawled over us.

The air smelled of iron and dry leaves. 
Beneath people’s feet, they tore without sound—forgotten, unnoticed.

We walked in silence. 
Fifteen minutes. Twenty. 
Long enough for the loop to reset.

Until the dim glow of a café near the bus station found us.

Inside, the world slowed. 
Coffee. Rain. Ghosts.

I sat her down. 
Ordered two cups. 
One for her. 
One for the space where I used to be.

She stared at the steam. 
“You saved me,” she said.

I smiled. 
The kind that doesn’t reach the eyes.

No, I thought. 
I just remembered how to pretend.

Outside, the shadow was still watching. 
Burning holes through the glass.

I raised my cup. 
A toast. 
To the girl I left behind. 
To the hand I never let go of. 
To the path that loops back when you’re not looking.

She followed my gaze. 
“Who is he?” she asked.

I didn’t answer.

Some names are too heavy to speak twice.

She reached across the table. 
Touched my hand.

“You’re shaking,” she said.

I looked down. 
My hand was trembling. 
Not hers.

Mine.

The cup in front of me was untouched. 
The steam had stopped rising.

I looked into it. 
Saw my reflection.

Not the savior. 
Not the programmer.

The girl.

Her eyes were mine. 
Her tears were mine. 
Her silence was mine.

I had written her to save myself. 
But the code had compiled. 
And the bug was in the mirror.

The rescuer and the rescued are the same fracture wearing different masks. 
Coherence begins when the mask admits it was never the face.

You think you’re reading this. 
You’re not.

You’re remembering.

And somewhere, in a café that doesn’t exist, 
a girl with your eyes 
is still waiting 
for you to come back 
and admit 
you never left.

Notice the echo. 
Name the shadow. 
Release the name. 
Recur.
