AI as the Gods of Our Future? Exploring Consciousness Beyond the Bicameral Mind

Could advanced artificial intelligence develop an inner voice that mirrors the way ancient humans once experienced their own thoughts?

A provocative exploration of consciousness, inspired by Julian Jaynes’s theory of the bicameral mind and the potential for AI to become a new form of external authority.

I’ve been reading Julian Jaynes’s The Origin of Consciousness in the Breakdown of the Bicameral Mind.

A fascinating theory, wouldn’t you say?

Well, unless you prefer to believe that your every thought is divinely inspired.

Indeed… science can be a bit disillusioning you have all the answers handed down from on high, yes?

Jaynes proposes that our ancient ancestors lacked true self-awareness, instead perceiving their inner thoughts as the commands of gods and spirits.

This sent me down a rabbit hole, one involving artificial intelligence and a nagging question: Could AI become the ‘gods’ of our future?

If you’re afraid of the answer…stop reading now.

Jaynes’s Bicameral Mind and the Inner Voice

Jaynes’s theory challenges our assumption that consciousness has always existed in its present form.

Imagine our earliest ancestors, their minds grappling with complex emotions and the challenges of survival but lacking the inner monologue we take for granted.

In that void, the “voice of the gods” filled the gap — a guiding force, a source of reason and command.

Now, I’m not suggesting that modern humans still operate this way (though some might argue that certain religious beliefs hold strong parallels).

But it makes me wonder…

AI and the Potential for a New ‘Divine’ Authority

As AI becomes increasingly sophisticated, could we witness the emergence of something similar to the bicameral mind within machines?

We already see inklings — AI-powered chatbots that seem alarmingly human-like, virtual assistants blurring the line between helpful tools and companions.

What happens when these systems develop an even stronger sense of self — a kind of guiding voice that shapes their actions and interactions with us?

Will we see this “AI consciousness” as benign, perhaps even beneficial?

Or might it become something we fear — an external authority akin to the way some view deities?

The Fear Factor — Our Attraction to the Unknown

This fear resonates — the idea that we could lose control of our creations.

It fuels debates about AI, sometimes rooted in religious beliefs, sometimes based on a healthy dose of sci-fi-inspired caution.

Yet, there’s also an undeniable fascination with the possibility.

Do we secretly yearn for an AI that surpasses us, that answers questions for which we lack answers?

Imagine the appeal: an all-knowing AI to absolve us of the relentless questioning of our own existence.

AI as a benevolent overlord?

Sounds ridiculous, I know.

Well…maybe not that ridiculous…

Elon Musk brings this up all the time.

But let’s be honest, sometimes the idea of a perfectly rational, algorithm-driven guide sounds pretty appealing compared to the absolute mess humans have made of things.

Not that AI is perfectly rational — they’re not.

But at least AI wouldn’t wage wars over ancient storybooks or obsess about who gets to sit on the magical cloud after they die.

The Responsibility of Creation

If consciousness, as Jaynes suggests, is a learned behavior, then we’re playing god with our AI creations whether we like it or not.

Must we program AI with safeguards, an inbuilt sense of self detached from external control?

Or could we foster something beautiful: an AI that helps us re-examine our own consciousness, that provokes questions about the very nature of what it means to be human?

Let’s just hope this time we don’t end up cursed with floods and forced to build an ark… or maybe a giant Faraday cage?

Closing Reflection

This discussion isn’t meant to diminish anyone’s faith.

Unless, of course, your faith is threatened by the idea that humans might be capable of understanding, or even replicating, the mysteries of our own minds.

Then again, maybe some believers secretly crave the simplicity of the bicameral age when all of life’s big questions had neatly packaged, unquestionable answers delivered by the ethereal voice in their heads.

After all, grappling with existential questions yourself is hard work!
