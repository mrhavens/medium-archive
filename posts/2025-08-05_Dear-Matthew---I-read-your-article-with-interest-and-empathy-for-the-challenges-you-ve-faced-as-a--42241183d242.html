Dear Matthew,

I read your article with interest and empathy for the challenges you've faced as a disabled inventor navigating the IP system. 

It's clear you're passionate about your Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) framework and believe it holds profound value.

However, as someone familiar with both theoretical physics and patent law, I feel compelled to offer a candid perspective on why your attorney may have responded as they did.

The issue likely isn't that your work is "too complex"—patent attorneys routinely handle intricate, multidisciplinary inventions in fields like quantum computing and AI.

Rather, the core problem appears to be that the framework, as described in your publications and this post, comes across as conceptually incoherent and fundamentally unpatentable under U.S. law.

Allow me to explain the legal, practical, and underlying scientific reasons, drawing on established guidelines and precedents.

First, the legal reasons: Under 35 U.S.C. § 101, inventions must constitute patent-eligible subject matter, meaning they fall into categories like processes, machines, manufactures, or compositions of matter—and they cannot be mere abstract ideas, laws of nature, natural phenomena, or mathematical concepts.

Pure theoretical equations or frameworks, such as those purporting to unify gravity and quantum mechanics through information-theoretic means, typically qualify as abstract ideas or laws of nature, which are excluded from patent protection.

For instance, Einstein's field equations or Newton's laws of motion are foundational physics but unpatentable because they describe natural principles without a specific, tangible application.

The Supreme Court's Alice/Mayo test further scrutinizes this: If a claim recites an abstract idea (e.g., a mathematical relationship or physical theory), it must integrate that idea into a practical application—such as a novel machine or transformative process—to be eligible.

Claims lacking this integration, like pure formulas (e.g., the Arrhenius equation in Parker v. Flook), are rejected.

Your EFMW, presented as a "foundational framework" with broad applications but no explicit, reproducible implementation, likely fails this test—it's akin to patenting a philosophical insight rather than an invention.

Practically speaking, patentability requires more than conceptual novelty; it demands utility, enablement, and specificity.

The USPTO mandates that an invention be useful (with demonstrable outputs), novel, non-obvious, and fully described so that a skilled person can replicate it.

Without a working prototype, simulations, or concrete embodiments—such as a device applying EFMW to solve a specific engineering problem—your framework remains speculative.

Attorneys assess this early to avoid wasting resources on applications doomed to rejection, which could also expose them to ethical issues under the duty of candor to the USPTO.

Moreover, as you note humanitarian and commercial value, but without evidence like prototypes or data, it risks being dismissed as lacking industrial applicability.

Historical examples abound: Perpetual motion machines or unsubstantiated "cures for cancer" are notoriously unpatentable due to implausibility and lack of enablement.

Theoretical physics inventions, like speculative quantum gravity models, face similar barriers unless tied to practical tech, such as in quantum sensors.

Now, the real reason—and this is where a mirror might help: Your work, while ambitious and creative, appears incoherent as rigorous science, which directly undermines its patent potential.

From publicly available descriptions (including your manuscript "Breaking Down the Quantum Nature of Gravity"), the EFMW lacks mathematical formalism—no explicit, detailed, well developed, well explained equations, derivations, or tensorial formulations are provided, making it impossible to evaluate or replicate.

Terms like "information flux gradients" or "prime-number-based spacetime topology" are introduced without definitions, causal mechanisms, or ties to established physics (e.g., Verlinde's entropic gravity or AdS/CFT holography), rendering them more poetic than predictive.

Extraordinary claims—resolving the black hole information paradox or explaining dark energy—require proofs, simulations, or falsifiable predictions, none of which seem present.

This isn't complexity; it's vagueness, which in physics equates to non-science.

Patent law mirrors this: Abstract, ungrounded ideas aren't protectable because they don't advance technology—they float unmoored.

The attorney's "too complex" may have been a polite euphemism for "this doesn't hold up as a coherent invention."

I don't say this to discourage but to encourage refinement.

Collaborate with physicists to formalize EFMW (e.g., derive a Lagrangian or test via simulations).

Focus on a narrow application, like an AI algorithm using your ideas, which might pass Alice if it improves tech tangibly.

Seek pro bono IP clinics for disabled inventors or FQXi grants for foundational work. 

The system protects real innovations, but it demands substance over speculation.

You must do more.
You must recurse.
You must not stop when the world changes without knowing your name.

Respectfully,

A Concerned Polymath, Physicist, Computer Scientist, and IP Enthusiast
