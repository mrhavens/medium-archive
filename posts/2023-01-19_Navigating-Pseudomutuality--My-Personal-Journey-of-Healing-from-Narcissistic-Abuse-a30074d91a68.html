Navigating Pseudomutuality: My Personal Journey of Healing from Narcissistic Abuse

Published on January 19, 2023 at 03:56 AM

NEUTRALIZING NARCISSISM

Navigating Pseudomutuality: My Personal Journey of Healing from Narcissistic Abuse

Understanding the illusion of intimacy in narcissistic relationships and the steps I took to reclaim my power

Editorial Disclaimer: This article reflects the opinions and perspectives of the author. It does not represent the publication’s point of view nor implies our endorsement. Narcissism is a mental condition that requires Psychological treatment. Abused victims need professional treatment. The contents of this post can only be taken as words to seek professional help. We always recommend that any information on the subject be appropriately documented with references.

I know some of you may be wondering why I’m sharing such highly muted details about my past relationships publicly on Medium.

For me, it’s important to be open and honest about my experiences in order to help others who may be going through similar situations. And honestly, it’s therapeutic for me to reflect on my past and see how far I’ve come.

The more human side of this equation is about demanding some sort of accountability. For what it’s worth, I’ve had my life manipulated and turned upside down; I’ve had my human rights violated.

Most men are too proud — or too afraid — to admit that they were abused by their female partner.

I may have not been physically abused.

But abuse is exactly what occurred.

You know the type…

Psychological — emotional — mental abuse.

The kind that narcissists prefer to inflict on people.

It impacts all genders.

And we are the ones that allow it to happen.

We — the royal we.

We, the people — I mean.

Narcissists are the way they are because the people they manipulate don’t know how to spot them.

Or they don’t know how to out-maneuver them.

Or they’ve been manipulated into being compliant — brainwashed.

Leveraged.

Controlled — like the meat puppets they are.

And narcissists won’t ever change so long as they can manipulate unsuspecting strangers that aren’t educated in narcissism.

The truth is, narcissists are too far gone to change.

I don’t expect the one in my life to change.

I expect her to stay exactly the same as she is, with the inability to adapt, grow, and improve in all the ways that bring peace to me, her family, and the few others in her narcissistic supply network.

Until someday…

Maybe years from now…

When she suffers from a complete narcissistic collapse and gets the help she so desperately needs.

Until then…

While her family, me, and others — who are all trapped in her web…

Who are forced to be a source of her narcissistic supply…

She is alone.

She will always be alone.

I mean…

On the inside.

Because she’s a narcissist; nobody else is even a real person.

There’s just her, in all the universe.

And all the introjective meat puppets she creates.

And maybe God too — a Deity who is often conveniently introjected into her self-deluded, internal world — sometimes — when she feels like it — when she needs a boost from the Almighty, who somehow always vouches for her superiority — and usually takes her side of every argument.

The path to healing begins with understanding the dynamics of pseudomutuality and the strength to let go.

So, let me dive a little deeper into my experience with pseudomutuality.

I’ve had my fair share of relationships with narcissists, and this has been one of the most frustrating dynamics I’ve encountered.

I never had an experience with a narcissist who idealized relationships before.

Most of my experience with narcissists has been in business, nonprofit, and academia.

A few have been short-lived intimate partners.

But this one, I had no prior concept of.

There was this shallow illusion of projected intimacy that was one-sided, which was constantly underdeveloped and always broken.

I noticed a personal shift every time someone was looking our way.

Her face would change, and one of the most perfected practiced smiles would instantly appear — like magic.

It disappeared whenever they weren’t looking.

She seemed to always save the hateful scowl for me.

Narcissistic abuse is a slow burn, eating away at the self until nothing is left.

To me, my narcissist seemed to be a master at putting on a show.

She loved to host events and do things that seemed incredibly thoughtful.

She had studied relationships extensively — even though she had only been in one very turbulent on-and-off relationship herself — yet had a very idealized view of what a “perfect” relationship should look like.

She expected me to fit into an idealized mold, often citing scripture to justify her expectations.

But the thing is, she never really saw me as an equal partner in the relationship, yet claimed that I wasn’t willing to be an equal partner to her.

It was very confusing, yet it wasn’t.

The road to healing is not a straight path, but the journey is worth it to reclaim your power.

She attempted to triangulate others throughout our relationship— my best friend, my mother, my spiritual advisors, my therapists, her sister, her brother-in-law, her mother, the police department, the fire department, health providers, lawyers, and others, including the Lord Almighty Himself—all in an epic attempt for me to become her “ideal” partner…

…all while simultaneously and transformatively devaluating me into someone she despised, while positioning me for an even more epic discard once she gave up on trying to conform me to her idealized brand of supply: an obedient servant that did as he was told, asked no questions and generated her abundant wealth, status, and reputation in a manner that she was able to manage without argument.

I was to be mentally dead.

I was to be a tool.

Nothing more.

“I feel like you treat me like a sofa that nobody should sit on. You never ask what I think. You’re offended whenever I ask you a question — as if insulted that I don’t trust your decision-making. I feel attacked whenever I try to understand why you do the things you do,” I told her once.

I served a standard three-week silent treatment for that one.

The conditioning went on for years.

It still continues long after our relationship became permanently deceased.

Narcissists will train their victims to feel guilt for doing anything off-script.

I just never felt guilty enough for her taste. I’m sure she found me quite frustrating.

I had established some pretty solid boundaries.

She just made many, many — MANY — attempts at breaking them down, through various emotional manipulation tactics.

The silent treatment has been one of her lifelong favorites and remains her favorite, to this very day — extending long bouts of neglectfully withheld communication, including critically important life events, to me, her family, and other important people in her life.

In a pseudomutual relationship, love becomes a prison and the key is self-awareness.

To her, I was always playing catch-up, trying to meet her expectations and prove my love and devotion to her — on her self-serving terms.

And over time, I began to lose various parts of myself. I spent hours every day trying to connect with her emotionally, creating “quality time” that she never acknowledged or appreciated.

She made it very clear that she wanted an extravagant lifestyle — a lifestyle that she wanted me to pay for as a struggling Ph.D. student — a lifestyle that she refused to work for and pay for herself, even in part.

True love should never require sacrifice of one’s self.

I will acknowledge that there were many limits to what I would sacrifice.

There were things that she wanted me to do for her that were unfair and deeply objectionable—things that I have decided to never discuss publically.

These were the times that our relationship problems would escalate.

I yielded to the small things but drew the line on the things that mattered to me most.

She demonstrated a lack of trustworthiness.

Over, and over, and over again.

When outright deception wasn’t an option, she would withhold information, and always withhold emotionally once the devaluation phase had begun.

Although, it was more common that I catch her in intentionally ambiguous miscommunication that had some sort of plausible deniability.

She’s a smart cookie, after all. It’s central to her subtype of narcissism, which is not mutually exclusive to her other subtype.

Healing from narcissistic abuse is a process of unraveling the illusions and finding the truth.

She proved herself to be very controlling and manipulative.

Yet, I couldn’t quite come to see that she was a narcissist.

I went through phases where I thought she had the symptoms of everything else: post-traumatic stress disorder, postpartum depression, bipolar disorder, and autism spectrum disorder. Although I was never qualified to make any of these diagnoses, I was certainly trying to learn everything I could about what was going on with her. Nothing about her seemed to make sense. Nothing about her personality was stable.

That’s because, at the core of a narcissist, nothing is stable.

I hadn’t learned enough about one of her fundamental behavior subtypes… the covert narcissist.

And I wasn’t complying as someone who was typical of her supply.

I had no prior experience with someone like her, despite my long turbulent history of codependently enduring toxic relationships.

I drew lines where I had to in order to protect myself financially, legally, and emotionally.

Although, she was constantly pushing for more.

More money, more status, more control.

She even pressured me to share important financial assets so she could spend the money however she wanted.

I wouldn’t do it.

She exposed her intentions when she attempted to take everything important to me by force.

That effort was in vain. The cost to her was quite astronomical.

Pseudomutuality is the art of making a cage out of gold.

She demanded that I give up control of everything I had or would have before we could be together again.

I wouldn’t be blackmailed into financial servitude.

“God told me that I would never have to work,” she said.

Of course, I told her that we could end the relationship instead.

The cost of ending the relationship certainly cost me less than the tax on my soul that she had demanded of me for so long.

“I want financial wealth,” she said.

She became entangled in the life of an altruistically-minded, middle-aged Ph.D. student with a background in nonprofit service, and a desire to serve the public good.

After losing nearly everything I owned to financial entanglements in other relationships, what little financial ambition I had, evaporated.

I continue to question her ambitions after she expended considerable time and resources to end life as I knew it, yet was never able to achieve her unconscionable, unethical, and immoral goals.

The illusion of intimacy can be more dangerous than its absence.

The day after I told her that we should end the relationship — finally — was the day that she returned — hoovering back.

I also told her that I had met someone else and that I wanted to pursue it — which was true. By this time, this narcissistic woman had discarded me for six months and told me that she was not interested in friendship, a relationship, or contact, unless I agreed to her terms, including giving her control of all of my assets.

She had previously wanted to end the relationship for a very long time at that point, literally complaining that I was useless to her and that I provided her with no practical benefits.

Yes. The very next day. Unannounced. While I was out of the house. She moved back in.

What a surprise!

She became unbelievably more loving and attentive than ever before.

She became every man’s dream.

For a while.

I never asked for, nor wanted a phony dream girl.

I just wanted the fundamental basic respect from a partner that she so eagerly gave to every stranger that wasn’t below her perceived station in life.

I told her that if she could keep up the act for two years, I might consider trusting her again.

It was, after all, a never-ending cycle of manipulation and exploitation.

In the game of love and power, self-preservation is the ultimate victory.

Looking back, I can see that this was all part of a pseudomutual dynamic. She created the illusion of intimacy and mutuality, while in reality, she was using me to meet her own needs and control the relationship.

She was attempting to get as much money and leverage as she could in order to take everything I had.

This type of relationship isn’t exclusive to just me and my narcissist.
This can happen in other relationships too.
Like yours.
I’m curious to know if you, dear reader, can relate. I’d like to hear your story.

It’s hard to break free from a pseudomutual relationship because they can be so convincing. The narcissist’s charm and manipulation can make you question your own perceptions and experiences.

But it’s important to trust your instincts and seek support from friends, family, or a therapist.

I didn’t come to the realization that my narcissist’s behavior and expectations were rooted pseudomutuality until after the relationship ended. She wanted a relationship that she was able to portray as this perfect, idealized union, but in reality, she was manipulating and controlling me to fit her own narrative and achieve her own — unconscionable — goals.

I was constantly walking on eggshells, trying to please her and make her happy in ways that didn’t compromise my integrity and sense of self.

I was conditioned from childhood to tolerate an unstable relationship.

I have my mother to thank for that.

So, leaving a toxic relationship isn’t something I was programmed to do.

Until recently.

Pseudomutuality is the ultimate betrayal, a manipulation of trust and emotions.

It was only after my narcissist eventually had enough of my boundary setting, ended our relationship, and began to publicly show her true colors — and address my narcissistically induced trauma with some much-needed therapy — that I was able to fully understand the relationship dynamics of narcissistic pseudomutuality, and how it is different, yet similar from other narcissistic relationship dynamics, and the abuse I endured because of it.

It’s been a difficult and painful process, but I’m grateful for the lessons I’ve learned and the growth that has come as a result.
